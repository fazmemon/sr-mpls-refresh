Multicast — the machine, not the flashcards

One organising idea and everything else falls out of it: two layers, two jobs. Routers build the tree (PIM, receiver toward source). Switches eavesdrop on it (IGMP snooping — they never speak PIM). Every multicast fault is one of those two layers failing, and the symptom tells you which. Your anchor: you ran exactly this machinery at carrier scale for IPTV — trading only raises the determinism bar.

Multicast machine1
The organising idea

Two layers, two jobs

Hold that split and most interview answers derive themselves: darkness on one switch = the eavesdropping layer; darkness everywhere on one feed = the tree; gaps on one host = the host. You don't memorise answers — the model hands them to you.
2 · Two layers2
The machine in motion

The story of one receiver joining feed A

  1. The host wants feed A, so it sends an IGMPv3 membership report: include source 10.10.0.1, group 232.1.1.1 — it names the source AND the group: the (S,G).
  2. The access switch overhears that report (snooping) and opens this port for the group. Eavesdropping, nothing more.
  3. The last-hop router sees it and sends a PIM (S,G) join toward the source — each router on the way installs state and passes the join up until it splices into the existing tree. The tree grows from receiver toward source, on the shortest path.
  4. Packets flow down the tree. Each router forwards a multicast packet only if it arrived on the interface facing the source — the RPF check. That is multicast's loop protection: no RPF, and any topology loop becomes a packet storm.
The overlooked worker — the querier. Snooped state is soft; it expires. Someone must keep asking the wire "who still wants what?" (the IGMP General Query) so hosts re-answer and switches refresh their port state. Normally the router does it. A VLAN with no querier rots silently — memberships age out group by group while packets keep arriving upstream. Design a querier onto every receiver VLAN; don't discover it at 3am.
3 · The join3
Why trading killed the rendezvous point

ASM vs SSM

Classic ASM — (*,G)

The receiver joins "anyone sending to group G." The network now has a matchmaking problem — receivers don't know the sources — so it needs a matchmaker: the RP, where senders register and the shared tree roots, then a switchover to the source tree.

The cost: shared state, shared fate, RP redundancy engineering, MSDP between RPs — a whole failure-mode family that exists ONLY to answer "who is sending?"

SSM — (S,G)

The receiver already knows the source — market-data vendors publish it — and names it in the join: (S,G), IGMPv3 include, the 232/8 range.

The matchmaking problem disappears, and with it the RP, the register process, the shared tree, and MSDP. What remains is the minimal machine: a shortest-path tree from each receiver to a named source. Deterministic by construction.

That determinism is why trading runs SSM — and it makes the A/B feed model trivial: two sources, two groups, join both (S,G)s, arbitrate by sequence number. Nothing shared between the planes, so nothing correlates their failures.
4 · ASM vs SSM4
The trading application of the machine

A/B feeds — redundancy at zero added latency

  • Feeds carry sequence numbers — take whichever of A/B delivers each sequence first, discard the duplicate. Loss on one line is invisible.
  • That's why retransmission never leads the design: a resend costs milliseconds; the B feed is already there.
  • Recovery ladder when BOTH gap: retransmit request (off the hot path) → snapshot/refresh channel (also how a late joiner starts).

The rule that compresses the table: darkness is a STATE problem, gaps are a DROP problem. Trees don't drop packets — they exist or they don't. Drops live in buffers — hosts and edges. Decide dark-vs-gappy first and you've picked the layer before touching a CLI.

The sharp edge + the boundary

No backpressure — and what stays off the SR fabric

  • Multicast has no backpressure. The feed never slows because one box is slow — a slow consumer gaps itself: its socket buffer fills and UDP drops locally while every other consumer stays clean.
  • Isolation: latency-insensitive consumers ride conflated streams (the price now, bounded staleness); the hot path gets its own rail; a bulk consumer never joins a raw full-depth group it can't drain.
  • Keep the SSM plane its own plane. EVPN-VXLAN and SR are unicast — don't tunnel a deterministic A/B feed through a controller-computed fabric. If convergence is ever mandated: Tree-SID (SR point-to-multipoint) is the EOS-supported path; BIER is cleaner but unproven there — verify before committing.
  • Ordering matters more than loss: per-flow ECMP is safe (one flow pins one path); per-packet spray is forbidden here — arbitration tolerates loss but reordering poisons gap detection.
The model in one breath: routers build the tree with PIM toward a source the receiver names; switches eavesdrop the IGMP conversation to know which ports care; a querier keeps that eavesdropped state alive; RPF keeps the whole thing loop-free. Break any one of the four and the symptom tells you which.

When it's posed as design

What the room grades

  • 1 · The arithmetic forces multicast — fan-out sizing first: hundreds of consumers of one fat stream make unicast infeasible at the SOURCE's egress. You didn't choose multicast; the numbers did.
  • 2 · SSM, named unprompted — (S,G) joins via IGMPv3, 232/8, no RP — and why: the RP failure-mode family bought nothing a published source list doesn't.
  • 3 · The querier as a design item — on every receiver VLAN, decided at design time.
  • 4 · RPF as the safety story — why a multicast plane doesn't loop.
  • 5 · A/B on physically disjoint planes + sequence arbitration above — with SRLG honesty about "disjoint".
  • 6 · Know the boundary — the deterministic SSM plane stays off the SR/VXLAN fabric.
Your anchor, said once: carrier IPTV ran exactly this machinery at scale — same PIM, same IGMP, same trees. The trading version raises the determinism bar and adds the arbitration layer on top. You're updating lived ground, not learning a new field.
