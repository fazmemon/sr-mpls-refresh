You read shapes, not packets

A capture is fifty thousand packets and nobody diagnoses by reading them. Every fault has a shape — a constant 40ms, a cohort of retransmits, a window that goes to zero, a burst that never appears in the utilisation graph. Learn ten shapes and you diagnose from the pattern in seconds.

And before any of that, one habit that separates senior from not: ask what the capture is lying to you about.

"First I'd confirm the capture point isn't the source of the symptom — a SPAN under load drops silently and looks identical to network loss."

Question zero, part two

The capture is lying to you

  • 64KB segments on a 1500-MTU link. Not a bug — TSO/GSO on transmit and LRO/GRO on receive mean the host hands the NIC one giant buffer and the NIC segments it. The super-frame never existed on the wire.
  • "Bad" checksums on everything outbound. Checksum offload — the NIC hasn't computed them yet when your local capture sees the packet.
  • Software timestamps are applied when the kernel sees the packet — after interrupt coalescing, NAPI batching and scheduling. Tens of microseconds of noise, useless for latency work.
The fix when it matters: capture on a tap, or disable offloads for the test (ethtool -K). Knowing this stops you chasing a phantom MTU problem for an afternoon.

Shape 1

Retransmission · out-of-order · duplicate

Three things that look similar and mean completely different things. Tell them apart by sequence number versus arrival time.

ShapeWhat you seeWhat it means
RetransmissionSame sequence, arriving after the gap — following an RTO or 3 dup-ACKsReal loss (or a lost ACK)
Out-of-orderA lower sequence arriving between higher ones, with a tiny time deltaNo loss at all — reordering. ECMP/parallel-path asymmetry. A design finding.
DuplicateSame segment twice, no gap to fillUsually your capture — the same packet mirrored from two ports
Why the distinction earns marks: calling reordering "loss" sends someone hunting a nonexistent fault. Out-of-order on a fabric means flows are being spread across paths with different latency — that's a load-balancing conversation, not a packet-loss one.

Shape 2

Recovering well vs falling off a cliff

A receiver that gets an out-of-order segment re-ACKs the last in-order byte — that's a dup-ACK. Three of them trigger fast retransmit. SACK blocks tell the sender exactly which ranges arrived so it resends only the hole. Lots of dup-ACKs is a stack coping. RTOs instead of dup-ACKs means loss so heavy there weren't enough packets left to trigger fast retransmit.

Shape 3

Zero window — the network did its job

ZeroWindow means the receiver told the sender to stop: its application isn't draining the socket buffer, so it advertised a window of zero. The sender then window-probes. The network delivered perfectly.

  • Causes: a stalled or slow application, a blocked thread, an undersized SO_RCVBUF.
  • Window Full is the other side of it — the sender has filled the advertised window and must wait for an ACK to free space.
  • The UDP twin: exactly the same disease with no backpressure — the kernel just drops, and it shows as RcvbufErrors in nstat/netstat -su. No zero-window warning, because UDP has no way to say "stop".
"Zero-window means the network delivered fine and the application couldn't keep up — I'd be looking at the consumer, not the path."

Shape 4 — the classic

A suspiciously constant ~40 ms

Request/response stalling by the same amount every time. Nothing is broken — two optimisations are deadlocking each other.

  • Nagle holds a small write until the previous small segment is ACKed (to avoid tinygrams).
  • Delayed ACK holds the ACK up to ~40 ms hoping to piggyback it on data going the other way.
  • Together: the sender waits for an ACK that is deliberately being delayed. Quantised ~40 ms stalls.
The tells in the capture: the delay is suspiciously constant rather than variable, it only affects small writes, and it disappears on large transfers. Fix: TCP_NODELAY, and write whole messages in one call instead of header-then-body.
Why it matters here: this is the canonical "our order path has a mysterious 40 ms" answer, and it's a host/application fault that gets blamed on the network every time.

Shape 5

Handshake fine, large transfers hang

  1. The three-way handshake completes and small packets flow — so people conclude the path is fine.
  2. Full-size segments with DF set are sent, repeatedly, and never ACKed.
  3. No ICMP fragmentation-needed comes back — because a firewall ate it.

That's a PMTU black hole. The sender never learns the real MTU and retransmits full-size forever.

Confirm it: a large ping with DF set, walking the size down until it succeeds. Fix: permit ICMP type 3 code 4, or MSS-clamp at the edge. The DC version: a VXLAN or VPN overlay adding ~50 bytes on a 1500-byte underlay produces exactly this signature — which is why overlays want a 9000-byte underlay.

Shape 6

RST — and the tell that impresses

  • Nothing listening on the port — connection refused.
  • Abortive close — the application used SO_LINGER 0 instead of a graceful FIN. Common in trading stacks that want the socket gone now.
  • A middlebox injecting it — a firewall tearing the session down. The tell: the TTL or IP-ID doesn't match the real peer's other packets.
  • No state for the connection — after a reboot, a failover, or a NAT/state-table timeout. This is the classic "idle sessions die after N minutes".
The middlebox-injection tell is the one worth having ready — spotting a forged RST by its TTL is the difference between blaming the application and finding the firewall.

Shape 7 — multicast

Proving where the tree broke

Work the path in order, and say what each capture rules out:

  1. At the receiver — is an IGMP membership report leaving it, and for v3 is it an INCLUDE (S,G)? A host that never joined is an application fault, not a network one.
  2. On the access switch — is snooping programming the port, and is there a querier on the VLAN? No querier → tables age out → it works, then dies after a couple of minutes.
  3. At the first-hop router — PIM. With ASM you should see the (*,G) join toward the RP, then the (S,G) switchover. With SSM there's no RP and no shared tree at all.
  4. Anywhere — RPF. The source must be reachable out the interface the traffic arrived on, or it's dropped silently. Asymmetric routing kills multicast on its own.
The structure is the answer — source → host stack → L2 snooping → RPF → hop-by-hop state. The commands are vendor trivia.

Shape 8 — the one they care most about

Market data gapped. Network or host?

Then the A/B test, which is the part that shows you've worked a market-data network: A and B are independent paths carrying the same sequence space. A gap on A only is a path problem and arbitration should have covered it. The same gap on A and B at the same sequence is at the source or on a shared segment — which also means your "disjoint" feeds aren't.

Shape 9

40% utilised and dropping

Utilisation counters are averages over the polling interval. A burst that hit line rate for 200 µs and overflowed the egress buffer is completely invisible in a 30-second average. The port is drop-free at the average and lossy at the instant.

  • Prove it: a Wireshark IO graph at 1 ms or finer, on a capture with hardware timestamps — the burst appears. Or read the box's queue-depth and drop counters instead of utilisation.
  • Cause: usually incast — many senders synchronising onto one receiver — or a speed mismatch, 100G into 10G.
  • Fix: buffer, pacing, or ECN. Not "add bandwidth" — the link isn't full.
This is the most common argument you will be asked to demolish — someone points at a graph showing 40% and concludes the network is fine.

Measuring, not just reading

Do you trust the timestamps?

  • Software timestamps — applied when the kernel sees the packet. Interrupt coalescing, NAPI batching and scheduling put tens of microseconds of noise on them.
  • Hardware timestamping on the NIC or tap — what you use when the number actually matters.
  • Comparing two capture points requires a shared clock discipline: PTP with hardware timestamping (boundary clocks ideally), not NTP. Without it the delta between two captures is meaningless.
"Before quoting a latency from captures I'd establish whether the timestamps are hardware and whether the two capture points are PTP-disciplined — otherwise the delta is noise."

Overlay and RDMA

VXLAN and RoCEv2 in a capture

VXLAN — UDP 4789

Outer MAC/IP/UDP → 8-byte VXLAN header (VNI) → the original L2 frame.

Check two things: the outer UDP source port, which is a hash of the inner flow and the only entropy ECMP has — if it's constant, all overlay traffic pins to one path. And MTU, per slide 9.

RoCEv2 — UDP 4791

InfiniBand transport headers over UDP. Look for the lossless machinery:

ECN marks + CNPs coming back = congestion being managed properly under DCQCN. PFC pause frames = you've reached the blunt instrument, with head-of-line blocking and deadlock risk.

The design point: ECN/DCQCN should be doing the work. PFC is the last resort — lots of pause frames in a capture is a finding, not a healthy fabric.

The method

What the room is grading

  • 1 · You question the capture before the network. Capture point, offload, timestamps — asked unprompted.
  • 2 · You reason from shapes, not by scrolling packets. Name the pattern, then confirm it.
  • 3 · You say what each observation rules OUT, not just what it suggests. That's diagnosis rather than guessing.
  • 4 · You separate "the network delivered it" from "the host lost it" — the single most valuable distinction in a trading environment, and the one most people can't make.
  • 5 · You don't confuse averages with instants. Utilisation lies; queue depth and ms-resolution don't.
