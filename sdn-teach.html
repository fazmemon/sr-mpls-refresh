SDN — three ideas wearing one name

The reason SDN feels slippery is that it isn't one thing. Three separate ideas got the same label, one of them died, and the other two are just called "the network" now. Hold that split and every SDN question becomes answerable — including the ones where the honest answer is "that approach lost, and here's why."

Positioning warning — the recruiter was explicit (22 Jul): they want hands-on people. They are not interested in "the architect who drove it". So every answer in this round lands on what you did with your hands — the model you worked through with the developers, the migrations you ran, the things you fixed when they broke at 2am. The decisions you made are context, mentioned after the doing, never instead of it.

The post-mortem that earns you credibility

Why "dumb switches, central brain" lost

  • The failure domain was catastrophic. If the controller is the brain, losing it means the network stops thinking. Thirty years of distributed protocol design existed precisely to remove single points of failure — OpenFlow put one back in.
  • It didn't scale. Flow-by-flow programming from a central point means controller round-trips in the forwarding path and flow-table entries as the scaling unit. Fabrics have millions of flows and finite TCAM.
  • The silicon never wanted it. Merchant ASICs are pipelines with fixed tables, not general-purpose match-action engines. OpenFlow assumed hardware that mostly didn't exist.
  • The problem was mis-stated. Operators didn't actually hate distributed routing — BGP and IS-IS work. What they hated was configuring boxes by hand, one CLI at a time. That's a management problem, not a forwarding problem — and idea 2 solved it.
"The industry mistook a management problem for a forwarding problem. Once that was clear, the answer stopped being 'replace the control plane' and became 'give the control plane an API'."

4 · The stack4
The thing you actually did at Sky

What a data model is, and why it changed everything

A YANG model is a typed schema for a network's configuration and state — a contract. Not a script, not a template: a definition of what a valid network looks like, in a form a machine can check.

Screen-scraping the CLI

Encodes syntax. Breaks when output formatting changes. No types, no validation. Vendor-specific by definition. You can push config but you can't meaningfully ask "is the network what I asked for?"

Model-driven

Encodes intent. Validated before it touches a box. Same intent renders to multiple vendors. Config and operational state in one vocabulary — so you can diff what you wanted against what is.

  • OpenConfig — models written by operators (Google and the hyperscale/carrier group), not vendors, precisely to escape per-vendor trees. This is the one you used.
  • Native models — the vendor's own tree. Richer, complete, and a lock-in. Most real estates run OpenConfig where it covers the feature and native where it doesn't.
"The hard part of model-driven networking isn't the tooling — it's the translation. Turning a network architecture into a schema forces you to make every implicit design decision explicit. That's where the work is."

The point to make: confirmed-commit is the underrated one. A change that automatically reverts if you don't confirm within N seconds means a config that cuts off your own management access repairs itself. That single feature is why NETCONF survives alongside gNMI.
6 · Transports6
Your strongest technical bridge

Streaming telemetry vs SNMP — and why a trading firm cares

SNMP polling

You ask, typically every 30–60s. Per-OID, UDP, and the box burns CPU building the reply. The answer is an average over the interval.

Streaming telemetry

The box pushes on subscription — sub-second, structured, over a persistent gRPC channel, on-change or sample-interval. Same YANG paths as config.

Why it matters here specifically: the congestion that hurts a trading network lasts microseconds. A microburst that fills an egress buffer and drops market data is completely invisible in a 30-second average — the interface will read 40% utilised while it is dropping. Polling cannot see the event that matters.

"You can't poll your way to microsecond visibility. If the events you care about are shorter than your polling interval, your monitoring is telling you a comforting story about a network that's dropping packets."
This is the slide that connects SDN to everything else you know — buffers, microbursts, incast, p99. Use it: it turns an SDN question into a conversation about the fabric, where you're strong.
7 · Telemetry7
The operating model

Source of truth, drift, and the reconciliation loop

  1. Intent lives in one place — a repo / IPAM / database. The network is a rendering of it, never the master. If the only record of why a box is configured that way is the box, you have no source of truth.
  2. The pipeline is the only write path. Render intent → validate against the model → stage → apply.
  3. Read the network back continuously and diff operational state against intent. This is the loop most people skip, and it's the one that makes the rest true.
  4. Drift is inevitable — someone fixes something on the CLI at 3am, and they were right to. The question is whether the system notices. Break-glass is fine; unrecorded break-glass turns your model into fiction.
Intent-based networking, demystified: it's this loop with a marketing name — declare desired state, render it, continuously verify, remediate the delta. Say it that plainly and you sound like you've built one rather than read about one.
8 · Source of truth8
The question they will definitely ask

"The controller dies. What happens?"

The correct architecture answer is fail-static: the data plane keeps forwarding on the state it already has. The controller's absence costs you change and visibility, never traffic.

What you lose

Ability to push changes. New provisioning. Telemetry aggregation. Central path optimisation.

What must keep working

Forwarding. IGP and BGP convergence. Fast reroute. Everything on the trade path.

The failure to name: any design where a controller outage drops packets is broken, regardless of how good the controller is. Blast radius of "we can't make changes for an hour" is survivable. Blast radius of "the fabric is down" is not.
"I'd design so the distributed control plane stays authoritative for forwarding, and the controller owns intent, provisioning and telemetry. Then a controller outage is an operational inconvenience, not an outage."
9 · Fail-static9
The design principle underneath all of it

Distribute forwarding · centralise intent

  • Why EVPN beat controller-per-fabric in the DC: EVPN is a distributed control plane — it converges with no brain, survives partition, and scales by protocol design. The controller-centric fabric managers that competed with it all had a central dependency EVPN doesn't.
  • What a controller genuinely adds is what no single router can see: intent, provisioning at scale, cross-domain policy, path computation with global constraints, and telemetry aggregation.
  • So the split is functional, not layered: forwarding decisions distributed, policy and intent centralised. Centralising forwarding re-creates the single point of failure that routing protocols spent decades eliminating.
This is the sentence to have ready: "Distribute what must survive a partition; centralise what benefits from a global view." Everything else in this round derives from it.
10 · The principle10
Where SDN meets your strong ground

Centralised path computation — and why SR is the SDN data plane

  1. BGP-LS carries the IGP link-state database northbound — nodes, links, TE attributes, SRLGs, SR SIDs. The controller gets a live topology without joining the IGP. It's a reporting protocol; you don't route on it.
  2. The PCE computes a constrained path over that topology — global view, so it can do what no single headend can.
  3. Push it back down via PCEP (stateful PCE keeps ownership and re-optimises) or BGP SR Policy (SAFI 73 — the path distributed as a route). Arista EOS has no PCEP — so on an EOS network the answer is BGP SR Policy or controller-driven config.
Why SR is the thing that made this work: the path is a stack of SIDs imposed at the head end — no per-hop signalling, no state in the core. The controller changes a path by talking to one router. RSVP-TE couldn't do that: every midpoint held state, so central control meant touching the whole mesh.
"Segment Routing is what made centralised path control practical. Put the path in the packet and the controller only has to talk to the edge — that's the property RSVP-TE never had."
11 · PCE / SR11
The operational maturity question

Automation lets you break everything, uniformly, instantly

  • Dry-run and diff before apply — always. The diff is the change review.
  • Canary → rings → fleet. One device, then one failure domain, then the rest — with automated health gates between rings so the pipeline stops itself rather than waiting for a human to notice.
  • Change budgets — never more than N devices or one failure domain per window. A pipeline that can touch everything at once eventually will.
  • Confirmed-commit / rollback timers so a box that loses management reverts on its own.
  • Guardrails in the model — policy that refuses a change which would, say, drain both spines at once. The model is where "physically stupid" gets caught.
  • Pre- and post-checks on every change: capture state before, compare after, fail loudly on a delta you didn't intend.
"The scary thing about automation isn't that it makes mistakes — humans make more. It's that it makes them uniformly, everywhere, at once. So the pipeline has to be more conservative than a human would be."
12 · Blast radius12
A small question with a senior answer

ZTP — and the bit most people miss

A box is racked and cabled with no config. It boots, DHCP hands it an option pointing at a script/image server, it pulls image and initial config, then registers with the automation. Fine — that's the mechanical answer everyone gives.

The question to raise unprompted: trust bootstrap. How does the box prove it's yours, and how does the server prove it isn't an impostor? Answer: secure device identity — a vendor-installed, TPM-backed certificate (802.1AR IDevID) — plus signed images and a voucher exchange (secure ZTP / BRSKI-style), rather than "trust whatever answers DHCP on this VLAN."

Raising that unprompted is the difference between an ops answer and an architecture answer. It also lands well at a firm where the network carries money.

The answer to "tell us about your SDN experience"

Your ground — lead with the doing

They want hands-on, not the architect who drove it. So do not open with "I made the build-versus-buy call" — that is the exact sentence that gets you filed under "manager". Open with what you were doing with your hands, and let the decisions come out as context later, if at all.
"At Sky I worked the model-driven transformation on OpenConfig. I was in the data model itself with the developers — taking the real designs and turning them into the schema — and I ran the migrations and troubleshot them when they broke."

Then, only when it's asked for, the surrounding context: the build-vs-buy assessment (in-house end-to-end orchestration won), deciding automation scope, designing the transition. Context, after the doing.

  • Take every question to the box. If they probe, go to a concrete thing you did: what the model looked like, what broke in a migration, how you found it, what the fix was. Specifics are the proof of hands-on; summaries are the proof of the opposite.
  • Your hands-on credibility is 25 years in the CLI, CCIE and JNCIE — that is the ground to stand on if the SDN questions run thin: "I'm the person who configures it and fixes it when it's broken."
  • Never cite lab work as experience. Labs are how you get fluent enough to reason in the room — they are not a credential and mentioning one to this panel reads as padding. If you know it, just answer; where the answer came from is nobody's business.
  • Never say "I oversaw" or "I owned" or "my team did". Say what you touched.
Do not claim: ACI, NSX, Contrail, or any controller product you haven't run — and do not claim you wrote the automation code, because the developers did. If asked directly — "I haven't run those products. My modelling work was OpenConfig — I was hands-on in the model and in the migrations, not writing the orchestration code." A clean "I haven't" costs you nothing; a bluff that unravels costs you the round.
