HRT · design & failure rounds

Whiteboard scenarios

They draw a topology, hand you one failure, and then keep breaking things while you're still talking. They are not testing whether you know a fact — they're watching how you think when the ground keeps moving: what you ask before you answer, what you rule out, whether you notice the second-order effect, and whether you say "I'd check X first" instead of guessing.

There are deliberately no model answers in this deck. Reading answers first destroys the drill. You get the topology and the escalation ladder — think about them while you're out; we run them live and I hold what good looks like.

The four moves, every time: 1 · Ask before you draw — what's the traffic, what's the requirement, what changed. 2 · State what you'd check and what it rules out. 3 · Answer the failure, then name the second-order effect before they ask. 4 · Say the trade-off out loud — every fix costs something.

Scenario 1 · the one most likely to come up

Inter-DC research WAN — it degrades, then it cuts

   LON ────────────── FRA          each site: redundant border pair
    │  \            /  │          research DCs, plain IP, OSPF + BGP
    │    \        /    │          bulk dataset + checkpoint movement
    │      \    /      │
   NYC ────────────── AMS         subsea on the transatlantic legs
"We move large datasets between these sites. The LON–NYC path just started running at 60% of the throughput it did yesterday. Nothing is down. Where do you start?"
  1. You find no loss on the interface counters. Now what?
  2. A capture shows retransmissions — but the timestamps say the segments arrived out of order, not lost. What does that change?
  3. The LON–NYC circuit now fails completely. Traffic reroutes via FRA–AMS. Walk me through what happens in the next 30 seconds.
  4. The reroute path is a third of the capacity and is now congested. What do you do today, and what do you change in the design?
  5. Your "diverse" backup was in the same duct for 200m of its run. How did that happen, and how would you have caught it?
  6. Repair estimate on the subsea leg is eleven weeks. Does that change your capacity answer?
What they're probing: ECMP/hashing behaviour with few large flows · reordering vs loss · convergence sequence · N−1 capacity planning · SRLG and physical diversity · sizing resilience to repair time rather than reflex N+1.

Scenario 2

Market data goes dark — one desk only

  venue ──▶ feed handler ──▶ core ──▶ leaf-A ──▶ desk 1   (fine)
                              │
                              └────▶ leaf-B ──▶ desk 2   (dark)
        A and B feeds, PIM-SSM, IGMPv3
"Desk 2 sees nothing. Desk 1 is fine. Same feed. Go."
  1. What's your first question, and what does the answer eliminate?
  2. You confirm the host is sending an IGMPv3 report. Where next?
  3. It worked this morning and died at 11:02. Does that change your suspect list?
  4. Now desk 1 goes dark too — but only the A feed. B is still good. What's your model now?
  5. You look upstream and the router has (S,G) state but the outgoing interface list is empty. What does that tell you?
  6. Someone suggests turning off IGMP snooping to "fix" it. What do you say?
  7. Design question: they want to move this feed onto the EVPN/VXLAN fabric to save two switches. Your answer?
Probing: whether you separate the L2 eavesdropping layer from the L3 tree · querier/snooping ageing as the "worked then died" signature · RPF · reading mroute state · A/B independence · and whether you'll defend keeping the deterministic feed off a shared fabric.

Scenario 3

The feed is gapping — and the network looks clean

"Traders report gaps in market data. Every interface on the path is under 40% utilised with zero drops. Prove where it's going."
  1. How do you establish whether the packets ever reached the host?
  2. The capture at the host NIC contains the missing sequence numbers. Now what?
  3. You fix the socket buffer and it improves but doesn't stop. Where next?
  4. Now show me it isn't the network — a switch counter says 40% and no drops. Why might that be a lie?
  5. You find the burst. What actually causes it, and what are your options?
  6. Someone proposes upgrading the link from 10G to 25G. Does that fix it?
  7. Same symptom, but this time seq N is absent from the host capture and absent on both A and B at the same sequence. What does that tell you?
Probing: network-vs-host attribution as a discipline · RcvbufErrors and ring overruns · why averaged utilisation hides microbursts · incast · whether "add bandwidth" is your reflex · and A/B shared-fate reasoning.

Scenario 4

GPU cluster fabric — a spine dies mid-training

   spine1   spine2   spine3   spine4        RoCEv2, 1:1 non-blocking
     │ \  /  │  \  /  │  \  /  │            8-GPU servers, rail-optimised
   ──┼──┼────┼───┼────┼───┼────┼──          all-reduce collectives
   leaf1  leaf2  leaf3  ...  leafN
"Spine 3 hard-fails during a training run. Talk me through the next minute."
  1. What converges, how fast, and what does the job actually experience?
  2. The fabric was running at 80% before the failure. What now?
  3. Job throughput drops by far more than 25%. Why might that be?
  4. You see PFC pause frames climbing on two leaves. Good or bad?
  5. Someone proposes disabling PFC because it's causing head-of-line blocking. Your call?
  6. Now a second leaf reports CRC errors — intermittently, on one uplink. What's the risk with cut-through switching?
  7. Design question: how would you have sized this so a spine loss is a non-event?
Probing: ECMP member withdrawal vs reconvergence · N−1 capacity for synchronised collective traffic · why all-reduce degrades non-linearly (the slowest link paces everyone) · PFC as last resort vs ECN/DCQCN doing the work · cut-through propagating corrupt frames.

Scenario 5

Half the traffic works. Half doesn't.

"After a change last night, roughly half of the flows between these two sites fail. The other half are perfect. Same source and destination subnets."
  1. Before any tooling — what does the shape of that symptom already tell you?
  2. What single test would you run to distinguish the main candidates?
  3. Changing the source port makes a failing flow work. What's your model now?
  4. You find one member of an ECMP bundle is dropping. How would you have caught it faster?
  5. Different version: it's not per-flow, it's only large packets that fail. Now what?
  6. Another version: it fails only in one direction. What class of device does that implicate?
Probing: that "intermittent" is often deterministic per flow · hashing onto a broken member · MTU/PMTU as the large-packet signature · asymmetric routing plus stateful devices · and whether you vary the tuple as a deliberate diagnostic.
The trap: calling it "intermittent" and reaching for a packet capture. It isn't intermittent, and counters get you there faster.

Scenario 6

Mid-migration — half the network is on SR

"You're four weeks into the IP→SR migration. Sites 1–3 are migrated, 4–6 aren't. A core link fails on the boundary between them. What happens?"
  1. Walk through what each half does, and what happens to traffic crossing the boundary.
  2. TI-LFA protects the migrated half. What protects the other half, and what's the gap?
  3. Now someone reports a transient loop during reconvergence. How is that possible, and what removes it?
  4. You need to roll back site 3 tonight. Is that possible? What makes it possible or not?
  5. What would you have staged differently so this failure was boring?
  6. They ask: "should we just do a big-bang cutover at the weekend instead?" Your answer.
  7. What's your abort trigger, and who decides?
Probing: coexistence during migration · microloops and why post-convergence-path repair removes them · reversibility as a design property · sequencing irreversible steps last · and whether you have abort criteria agreed before the window rather than invented at 4am.

Scenario 7

The venue link, and the leak

   exchange ──▶ edge-1 ─┐
                        ├──▶ colo fabric ──▶ trading hosts
   exchange ──▶ edge-2 ─┘
        eBGP to the venue, strict filters
"Edge-1's BGP session to the venue is flapping every few minutes. Traffic keeps moving between edge-1 and edge-2. Start."
  1. What do you check first, and why is the flap worse than a clean failure?
  2. You confirm the physical link is clean. What else drops a session on that cadence?
  3. Someone suggests enabling route-flap damping. Your response?
  4. Now the venue accidentally advertises a full table. What happens in your network, and what should have stopped it?
  5. Your max-prefix has shut the session. Is that the right behaviour here?
  6. How do you detect the next failure in milliseconds instead of on the hold timer — and what's the risk of that?
  7. Design question: should this venue path be in the same routing domain as the research WAN?
Probing: flap vs failure · max-prefix as the thing that actually saves you · damping's deprecation and why · BFD with the caveat that aggressive timers cause the outage they prevent · and blast-radius separation between the trade path and everything else.

Scenario 8

The automation pushed it everywhere

"An automated change went out at 09:00. By 09:02 you've lost management access to forty switches. Go."
  1. First thirty seconds — what do you do?
  2. The data plane is still forwarding. Does that change your urgency, and how do you say that to a panicking room?
  3. What mechanism should have made this self-repair without anyone touching it?
  4. Why did it reach forty devices at all? Name the controls that should have stopped it at one.
  5. The pipeline reported success. How is that possible, and what does a real post-check look like?
  6. You get it back. What's the change you make so this class of failure can't recur?
  7. Now argue the other side: someone says this proves automation is too dangerous for the trading network.
Probing: confirmed-commit / rollback timers · canary and ring deployment with health gates between rings · change budgets · what a post-check must actually verify · and whether you can defend automation on blast-radius grounds rather than enthusiasm.

Scenario 9

The overlay that broke the big transfers

"We added an EVPN/VXLAN overlay last month. Since then, small flows are fine but large file transfers between two racks hang. Ping works."
  1. What's your immediate hypothesis, and what confirms it in one test?
  2. You confirm it. Why does ping succeed and the transfer hang?
  3. What are your three fix options, and which is the real fix versus the mitigation?
  4. Separately, you notice all overlay traffic between two leaves is using one uplink out of four. Why?
  5. What controls that, and what would you look at in a capture to prove it?
  6. Now the same fabric carries a multicast feed via ingress replication. What breaks as you add VTEPs?
Probing: the ~50-byte encapsulation and PMTU black-holing · why ICMP filtering makes it silent · MSS clamp as mitigation vs 9000 underlay as the fix · the VXLAN outer UDP source port as the only ECMP entropy · and ingress replication scaling with VTEP count.

Scenario 10 · the open-ended one

Design it from nothing

"We're building a new research site. Two hundred racks of GPU, and it needs to talk to our existing sites and to the trading colos. Whiteboard it."
  1. What do you ask before you draw a single box? (Get to at least six questions.)
  2. Draw the intra-site fabric. Justify the topology and the oversubscription.
  3. Now the WAN attachment. What's different about the colo path versus the research path?
  4. Where do the failure domains sit, and why there?
  5. They want it managed as code from day one. What does that add to the design?
  6. Halve the budget. What goes?
  7. Now defend the thing you cut, if they push back.
Probing: whether you ask before you design · requirements driving topology rather than fashion · deliberately different rules for the trade path · failure-domain placement as an explicit decision · and whether you can make and defend a trade-off under pressure rather than gold-plating.
The trap: drawing immediately. The questions you ask first are the answer to this one.

How to run these

The habits that get graded

  • Ask first, always. Two or three questions before you commit to anything. Someone who designs before asking is the person who builds the wrong thing.
  • Narrate the elimination, not just the conclusion. "I'd check X — if it's clean that rules out the whole physical layer" beats naming the right answer with no reasoning.
  • Volunteer the second-order effect. Answer the failure, then say what it causes next, before they ask.
  • Say the cost. Every fix trades something. Naming it unprompted is what separates senior.
  • Push back when the suggestion is wrong — damping, disabling PFC, adding bandwidth for a microburst. They will offer you bad ideas deliberately.
  • "I'd check that rather than guess" is a strong answer. Inventing a number or a fact is the only truly bad one.
  • Open with what you'd do, not what you decided or oversaw. Present tense, hands on the problem.
