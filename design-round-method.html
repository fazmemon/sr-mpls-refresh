The design round — drill the attack, never the scenario

You've done ~100 design interviews; the design body is lived ground. What this deck holds is the attack made explicit — the order, the sizing reflex, and the judgment through-line — so it fires cold into ANY scenario they pose. The reframe that unlocks it: a design round is your day job narrated out loud. Only the structure is new, and the structure is four moves.

Design-round attack1
The spine — order IS the seniority signal

Four moves, in order, every time

  • Scope in the trading register: what traffic — one-to-many market-data / many-to-one order flow / control? Latency and loss profile — deterministic or throughput? Scale — nodes, rate each, one DC or across colos?
  • The senior move: don't ask open questions — propose the requirements and confirm: "I'll assume market-data distribution, N consumers, latency-critical, gap-free — right?"

Your self-named rust — made reflex

Size it: the numbers force the architecture

The worked example (own it cold)

300 consumers × 10G of one fat stream = 3T of duplication — and the wall is the SOURCE's egress (one host cannot produce 300 copies), not the switch fabric. You didn't choose multicast; the arithmetic did.

Reflex numbers (keep hot)

1 ms ≈ 200 km in fibre · air/microwave ≈ 300 km/ms (1.5× fibre) · bytes × 8 = bits · a 100G NIC ≈ 12.5 GB/s · serialisation = header bits ÷ line rate.

The two-buffers seam (your own realisation — HFT panels probe exactly this): BDP = bandwidth × RTT = data in flight = the TCP window / socket buffer needed to keep a long fat pipe full — too small and the pipe starves (a throughput problem). The router egress buffer is different: packets queued at an output port, fills on microburst, tail-drops (an overflow problem). BOTH are sized bandwidth × delay — that's why they pattern-match: 100G × 60 ms = 6 Gbit ÷ 8 = 750 MB window; 50 ms × 100G ≈ 625 MB egress buffer. Same product, two different problems.
Worked example: 50,000 messages a second at 100 bytes = 5 MB/s = 40 megabits. Bandwidth is trivial; determinism and the message rate drive the design, not throughput.
3 · Sizing3
Where 25 years wins — structured, not recalled

Deep-dive: the failure-mode sweep

  • Run the sweep in a fixed order so nothing hides: single link · single node · the SHARED-fate pair (SRLG — "two of everything" in one duct is one of everything) · the slow consumer · the control-plane loss (does it degrade to "no changes" or to "no forwarding"?) · the operational failure (who fat-fingers what).
  • Quantify the failover: what carries the load the instant it breaks (pre-installed repair, ECMP survivors) vs after reconvergence — and does the surviving capacity actually hold the inherited reroute (the N-1 budget question).
  • Validate before capex — "why has nobody complained?" is a design input: if the pain isn't real, the build isn't justified. Commercial judgment is screened for.
  • Monitoring closes it: say what you'd watch — tail percentiles not averages, one-way latency (PTP-disciplined), the per-stage drop counters.
Whiteboard discipline: draw first, talk to the drawing — you design against a picture, not prose. Happy path fully drawn BEFORE any failure talk. When corrected on a hard fact, concede fast and fold it in; otherwise hold your line.

5 · The SR call5
Vary the surface, never repeat the rep

The eight surfaces the attack must fire into

  • Market-data distribution — fan-out → multicast, A/B + sequence arbitration, slow consumer
  • Order-entry / gateway path — asymmetry: multicast in, reliable unicast out; risk inline; kill switch; the RATE drives it, not bandwidth
  • Inter-DC / colo transport — latency budget picks the medium (microwave vs fibre vs λ), diversity SRLG-audited
  • Time distribution (PTP) — why, fan-out, accuracy budget, failure modes
  • Risk / kill-switch fan-in — stateless inline vs stateful beside the path; fail-closed; blast radius
  • Exchange connectivity — cross-connect pairs, session failover, drop copy, cancel-on-disconnect
  • SR-earns-it design — per-class steering / TI-LFA / consolidation on ground you own
  • Hub-and-spoke at scale — push the failure: hub = SPOF + bottleneck → regional hubs / hierarchy
The law: what becomes reflex is the ATTACK — scope → size → draw → break — never any one scenario. Memorised scenarios break on a new case; the varied rep transfers. Each rep = a fresh surface, cold, timed.

How it lands in the room

Delivery — the half that isn't networking

  • ~90 seconds per answer — land the point, then offer to go deeper. Rambling is the failure mode they name.
  • You lead. Set the agenda, state assumptions, propose the architecture, invite them to pick the deep-dive, lead that subdiscussion — your natural mode; the clock is the only new constraint.
  • Never STAR a technical question — structured diagnosis for technical, STAR for "tell me about a time" only.
  • Calm narration IS the test — for a trading-infra seat, composure while working a live problem is half the grade.
  • Defer on their network. The verified facts you hold: massively multi-AS · deploying BGP communities · IGP is OSPF (as far as you know) · eBGP got a raised eyebrow. Assert nothing beyond that — ask.
