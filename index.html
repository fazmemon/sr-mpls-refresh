Step 1 ŌĆö fibre availability & diversity

Demand wants 8 links ŌĆö but you build only where fibre runs, and cost is subsea Ōē½ terrestrial Ōē½ metro. The trap is diversity: every path you lean on for N-1 must be physically separate. LON's links to AMS and FRA both cross to the continent ŌĆö if they share a Channel duct or landing station, one anchor-drag or backhoe cuts both at once and Step 4's AMSŌåÆLONŌåÆFRA reroute never happens. "Diverse" counts only after you've SRLG-audited the ducts and landings ŌĆö not when it merely looks separate on the map.

Step 2 ŌĆö heavy pairs become direct links

The 8 heavy pairs ŌåÆ LON as a hub (4 links to its highest-demand cities: AMS, FRA, PAR, MAD) with an outer chain AMSŌĆōFRAŌĆōMILŌĆōPARŌĆōMAD carrying the rest. Every node has Ōēź2 links, so it stays connected through any single failure ŌĆö though connected isn't the same as having the capacity for the reroute (Step 3). And it's 8 links, not the 15 of a full mesh.

Step 3 ŌĆö light pairs don't get a link, they transit

AMSŌåöMAD is only 40G ŌĆö not worth its own link. It rides links you already built: AMS ŌåÆ LON ŌåÆ MAD (green). Do that for all 7 light pairs and every demand rides on 8 links, not 15 ŌĆö the heavy links absorb the transit. The matrix decided which links exist.

Step 4 ŌĆö the N-1 budget: a built link fails

The 220 on a link is the demand, not the load. LONŌĆōAMS actually carries 350G at rest ŌĆö 220 direct + 90 (AMSŌĆōPAR) + 40 (AMSŌĆōMAD) transiting it. AMSŌĆōFRA (250G) fails ŌåÆ AMS reaches everything only via LON, dumping AMSŌĆōFRA's 250 + AMSŌĆōMIL's 70 onto LONŌĆōAMS ŌåÆ 670G; LONŌĆōFRA 335 ŌåÆ 620G. Both ~1.6├Ś over a 400G link. Normal peaked at 355G, so the topology passes at rest and collapses on a single failure ŌĆö which is why you fix the N-1 budget before the topology and size for own load + worst inherited reroute.

Step 5 ŌĆö what N-1 actually costs

Each link must hold its own load plus the worst reroute it inherits ŌĆö the grey bar (490ŌĆō700G). At rest nothing tops 355G, so to survive one failure you buy ~1.9├Ś the bandwidth you use (4800G of link to carry 2520G). Backbones absorb this with dual planes ŌĆö every PoP homes to two disjoint planes, so a whole plane can fail and you still hold N-1. That part is universal ŌĆö every tier-1 runs it. What differs is how the reroute splits across the planes: today it's mostly IGP/ECMP even-split + capacity headroom; SR-TE / UCMP weighted paths split it proportionally so no fat pipe sits idle ŌĆö the efficiency win, but most operators haven't deployed SR yet. The matrix gave you the links; the failure budget gave you the capacity and the planes.

Where EVPN came from ŌĆö pseudowire ŌåÆ VPLS ŌåÆ EVPN

The arc: point-to-point → multipoint-but-flood-and-learn → multipoint with MACs in the control plane. EVPN won by carrying MAC/IP in BGP (like it already does for IP), adding all-active multihoming + IRB, and running over MPLS or VXLAN — which is exactly the DC fabric. (Ran the VPLS→EVPN transition first-hand.)

Every leaf connects to every spine; spines never interconnect, leaves never interconnect. Any serverŌåÆserver = leafŌåÆspineŌåÆleaf = uniform 2 hops, with massive ECMP (one path per spine). No STP ŌĆö it's all L3. Add spines for bandwidth, add leaves for ports.

Underlay vs overlay ŌĆö the two stacked layers

graph TB subgraph OV["OVERLAY ŌĆö tenant view"] direction LR SA["Server A"] -.->|"VXLAN tunnel (EVPN-signalled)"| SB["Server B"] end subgraph UL["UNDERLAY ŌĆö physical IP fabric"] direction LR LA["Leaf1/VTEP"] --- S["Spine"] --- LB["Leaf3/VTEP"] end OV -. "rides on top of" .-> UL

Underlay (bottom): plain IP, eBGP carries only VTEP loopbacks. Overlay (top): the VXLAN tunnel between two VTEPs, with EVPN telling each VTEP where every MAC lives. The tenant thinks A and B share a LAN; physically it's IP + encapsulation.

The spine is a pure IP router ŌĆö it forwards on the outer VTEP-to-VTEP IP and is oblivious to tenants/VNIs. All the intelligence is at the leaves (the VTEPs). EVPN pre-populated Leaf1 with "MAC-B is behind Leaf3's loopback", so there's no flooding.

IRB ŌĆö symmetric vs asymmetric

graph TB subgraph A[Asymmetric IRB] A1["Ingress leaf: route into dest L2VNI"] --> A2["Egress leaf: bridge to host"] end subgraph S[Symmetric IRB] B1["Ingress leaf: route into L3VNI"] --> B2["Egress leaf: route out to host"] end

IRB = routing + bridging at the VTEP, with an anycast gateway (same IP+MAC on every leaf ŌåÆ gateway always local, seamless mobility). Asymmetric needs every VNI on every leaf; symmetric uses one common per-VRF L3VNI ŌåÆ scales, the modern default.

Spines carry the eBGP/OSPF underlay (every VTEP loopback reachable). Leaves are VTEPs running BGP-EVPN overlay. Hosts split across two tenants (VRFs). VXLAN tunnels run leaf Ōåö leaf.
