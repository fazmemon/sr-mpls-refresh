A · Spine-leaf / CLOS design

A1. Why did DCs move from 3-tier (access/agg/core) to spine-leaf, and what specifically breaks in 3-tier that spine-leaf fixes?

Traffic went north-south → east-west (VM-VM, GPU-GPU, distributed storage). 3-tier oversubscribes the core and leans on STP (blocks links, one active path). Spine-leaf is routed (L3) with ECMP — every leaf→leaf is a uniform 2 hops, all uplinks active, no STP, scale bandwidth by adding spines. Weak answer stops at "more bandwidth" and misses the STP/uniform-latency/all-active points.

A2. 4 spines, 32 leaves, each leaf has 4×100G uplinks (one per spine). A spine fails — quantify the impact. Then: what caps how many leaves you can add, and how many spines?

Lose 1 of 4 spines = lose 25% of fabric bandwidth, zero connectivity loss (ECMP reweights to 3 spines). Max leaves = spine port count; max spines = leaf uplink count (4 here). Bisection = n_spines × uplink_speed. To grow past that → 5-stage Clos (pods + super-spine) for host count, or multi-plane for bandwidth on fixed silicon.

A3. Where do you set oversubscription in a leaf-spine fabric, and what ratio for a trading fabric vs general compute?

At the leaf uplink:downlink ratio (sum of host-facing vs sum of fabric-facing). Trading/AI = 1:1 (non-blocking); general compute up to 3:1. Spine is non-blocking by construction. Weak answer says "the spine" — wrong, spine isn't where oversub is set.

B · Underlay (eBGP / OSPF, ECMP)

B1. Why is eBGP (RFC 7938) the dominant DC underlay over OSPF/IS-IS, and what's the ASN plan?

eBGP: no flooding domain (scales by ASN, contained blast radius), per-hop policy control, same protocol as the EVPN overlay. ASN plan: unique private ASN per leaf (64512–65534 / 4-byte), shared ASN across all spines (so a leaf sees the same AS from every spine → keeps paths as ECMP, and prevents a leaf's route looping back through another leaf). Weak answer misses why spines share an ASN.

B2. What does eBGP unnumbered (RFC 5549/8950) buy you, and what's actually carried?

No per-link /31s to plan — BGP peers over IPv6 link-local (auto, via RA), advertises IPv4 NLRI with an IPv6 next-hop. Saves an addressing plan across hundreds of fabric links; the host routes are still IPv4 /32 loopbacks. The underlay's only job is loopback (VTEP) reachability + ECMP.

B3. Leaf1→Leaf3, 4 spines, all advertise Leaf3's loopback equal-cost. One spine's path goes stale on a link flap — what keeps the other three usable instantly, and what's the failure if ECMP isn't there?

Multipath install of all 4 next-hops; a flap withdraws one, the FIB keeps 3 — no reconvergence needed for the survivors. Need maximum-paths/multipath-relax (eBGP paths differ in AS_PATH first-hop ASN → multipath-relax required to treat them equal). Without it: one best-path, no spread, flap = reconverge.

C · VXLAN dataplane

C1. Walk a frame Server-A→Server-B across the fabric. What does the spine see, and what's the encap byte-by-byte?

Ingress leaf (VTEP) looks up B → behind Leaf3's VTEP loopback → wraps: [outer IP src=ingress-VTEP dst=egress-VTEP | UDP dst 4789, src=hash(inner flow) | VXLAN hdr + 24-bit VNI | original L2 frame] ("MAC-in-UDP"). Spine routes only the outer IP — never sees tenant MAC/VNI. Egress VTEP decaps, delivers. Outer-src-UDP = inner-flow hash → gives spines ECMP entropy.

C2. VXLAN adds ~50 bytes. What MTU do you set on the underlay and what's the symptom if you forget?

Underlay MTU must cover original 1500 + ~50 VXLAN/UDP/IP overhead → set jumbo (9216) fabric-wide. Forget it → silent path-MTU blackhole / fragmentation on encap, intermittent large-frame loss while pings work. Classic gotcha.

C3. What gives the spines hash entropy when every flow between two VTEPs has the same outer IP pair?

The outer UDP source port = hash of the inner flow (5-tuple). Same VTEP pair, different inner flows → different src-port → spines ECMP across uplinks. Without it all VTEP-to-VTEP traffic polarises onto one spine path.

D · EVPN control plane

D1. Name the 5 EVPN route types and the one-line job of each.

T2 MAC/IP advertisement (host reachability, ARP/ND suppression, MAC mobility). T3 Inclusive Multicast (IMET — per-VNI BUM flood list). T5 IP Prefix (L3/inter-subnet, symmetric IRB, summarisation, external). T1 Ethernet A-D (multihoming: per-ES mass-withdraw, per-EVI aliasing). T4 Ethernet Segment (DF election). T2/3/5 everyday; 1/4 appear once you multihome.

D2. RD vs RT — what does each do, and which one is the tenant-isolation lever?

RD (Route Distinguisher) prepended per-EVI/VRF so overlapping tenant MAC/IP stay unique in BGP (often auto RID:VNI). RT (Route Target ext-community) controls import/export = which VRF/EVI a route lands in = tenant isolation. Flip an RT and the route stops importing on the far leaf — that's the macro-seg lever.

D3. AFI/SAFI for EVPN, and where do the route reflectors sit in an iBGP-EVPN design?

AFI 25 / SAFI 70. iBGP-EVPN: spines as route-reflectors (leaves are RR-clients), or eBGP-EVPN where the underlay eBGP also carries the EVPN AF. Spines reflect overlay routes but never become VTEPs in the classic design.

E · IRB (the one to nail)

E1. Symmetric vs asymmetric IRB — mechanism, and why symmetric scales.

Asymmetric: ingress leaf routes into the destination's L2VNI, egress just bridges (route-then-bridge). Needs every VNI configured on every leaf → doesn't scale. Symmetric: ingress routes into a common per-VRF L3VNI (transit VNI), egress routes out of it (bridge-route → L3VNI → route-bridge). Only the L3VNI must be everywhere, not every L2VNI → scales, the modern default. Weak answer can define both but misses "asym needs all VNIs everywhere."

E2. What is the anycast gateway and why does it make host mobility seamless?

Same virtual IP + virtual MAC configured on every leaf for a subnet → a host's default gateway is always the local leaf, identical everywhere. Move the host to any leaf and its gateway IP/MAC is unchanged → no ARP re-learn, no hairpin. Pairs with symmetric IRB.

E3. Centralized routing (one spine routes all inter-subnet, leaves L2-only) — when is it used and what's the EOS gotcha?

Lesser-used contrast to distributed IRB — bigger blast radius (all inter-subnet hairpins to the central router), the central node does its own ARP. EOS gotcha (verify in lab): the central router needs redistribute router-mac system ip on the MAC-VRF or it won't advertise host MAC+IP and routed traffic floods. Use as the "why distributed/symmetric wins" contrast.

F · Multihoming (ESI-LAG)

F1. A server is dual-homed to leaf1+leaf2 (all-active). Three EVPN mechanisms make that work — name them and what each prevents.

T4 (Ethernet Segment) → DF election: one leaf is Designated Forwarder for BUM toward the host → prevents duplicate broadcast/multicast delivery. T1 per-EVI → aliasing: remote leaves load-balance unicast to the host's MAC across both leaves even though only one advertised the T2 → uses both uplinks. T1 per-ES → mass-withdraw: on a leaf-host link failure, one withdraw clears all MACs on that ES → fast convergence (vs per-MAC withdraw). This is the standards-based replacement for MLAG/vPC.

F2. How is the DF elected, and what changed in RFC 8584?

Original RFC 7432: modulo(VLAN-id, number-of-PEs-in-ES) ordered by PE IP — deterministic but can imbalance and causes churn on membership change. RFC 8584 HRW (Highest Random Weight / "service carving"): hash-based, minimises reassignment when a PE joins/leaves and spreads DF role better. Weak answer just says "lowest IP."

G · BUM / replication

G1. How does broadcast/unknown-unicast/multicast cross a VXLAN fabric, two methods, which is the DC default?

Ingress replication (head-end replication) — source VTEP unicasts a copy to each remote VTEP in the T3/IMET flood list; no multicast underlay needed → DC default. Or multicast underlay (PIM) — VNI→mcast group, fabric replicates; scales BUM better but needs PIM everywhere. ARP/ND suppression cuts most BUM anyway (VTEP answers locally from EVPN-learned MAC/IP).

H · MAC mobility / loop protection

H1. Host moves leaf2→leaf5. How does EVPN converge without a loop, and what stops a flapping host melting the fabric?

New leaf advertises a T2 with an incremented MAC-Mobility ext-community sequence number; highest seq wins, old route withdrawn → deterministic, no loop. Duplicate-MAC detection: N moves in M seconds (default e.g. 5 in 180s) → freeze + alarm (mis-cable/loop). sticky-mac pins a MAC so it can't move (security). Weak answer misses the sequence number as the tiebreak.

I · Cross-vendor interop (Juniper ↔ Cisco) — the Juniper edge

All three: both vendors RFC-compliant, different defaults. This is the failure class that humbles single-vendor engineers — exactly the Juniper-interview differentiator.

I1. Junos QFX ↔ Cisco Nexus EVPN: BGP session Established, but PfxRcd stuck at 0 and the log says "Received invalid Ethernet Tag ID 10999." Diagnose and fix.

Junos vlan-aware MAC-VRF stuffs the VNI into the Ethernet Tag; NX-OS VLAN-based service wants Tag 0 and silently drops anything else → the type mismatch is two different EVPN service interface types (RFC 7432), both legal. Fix: align service type — service-type vlan-based (or make both vlan-aware bundle). This is a service-model mismatch, not a transport bug.

I2. Sessions up, Type-2s present on the wire, but routes never import across the eBGP boundary. Why, and the two-part fix.

Auto-RT derives from the local AS (AS:VNI), so the two sides derive different RTs across an eBGP boundary → import never matches. Fix: pin explicit RTs on both ends (same import/export), and remove rewrite-evpn-rt-asn which silently mangles the AS portion of the RT during import matching. Weak answer says "RT mismatch" but misses the rewrite knob.

I3. ARP resolves both ways, MACs cross the fabric, but unicast still drops — traffic is routing around the L2 stretch instead of bridging across it. Root cause and fix.

An IRB subnet leaked into the underlay; the resulting BGP/underlay route (AD 20) beat the EVPN host route (AD 250) → the box politely L3-routed around the stretch rather than bridging across it. Fix: if no L3 gateway is needed there, pull the IRB, keep it pure L2 and let the routers route. The tell: bridging "works" at control plane but dataplane prefers an L3 path. This is overlay/underlay hygiene, not interop.

J · Hardware / silicon internals (your new ask)

J1. Two buffer architectures in DC silicon — name them, the chips, and which goes spine vs leaf and why.

Shallow on-chip shared-memory (Broadcom Tomahawk, Trident) — MB-scale, sub-µs, cut-through-capable → leaf and low-latency/AI spine. Deep off-chip HBM (Broadcom Jericho/J2/J2c+ DNX, Cisco SiliconOne, Nokia FP5) — 100s MB–GBs, ~µs access, VOQ + cell-based fabric → WAN-facing/peering/DCI or deep-buffer spine (absorbs incast/RTT×BW). Pick wrong: HBM in a low-latency leaf over-buffers (queue latency kills RoCE/incast); shallow in peering = 50ms×100G = 625MB needed, 165MB → drops.

J2. Walk a packet through a VOQ/cell-based chassis switch, ingress port → egress wire. Use the terms cells, pointers, VOQ, credits, fabric.

1. Ingress: port → parser → forwarding lookup (L2/L3/EVPN) → resolves egress port + queue.

2. Packet written to the ingress packet buffer; enqueued onto the VOQ = a virtual output queue held at ingress, one per egress-port/queue (not at egress).

3. Packet segmented into fixed-size cells; cells stored in buffer, tracked by pointers/descriptors (the packet isn't moved again — pointers are).

4. Egress scheduler issues credits/grants; on grant, cells are sprayed across all fabric links/planes (load-spread) toward egress.

5. Egress reassembles cells into the packet (pointer chain), egress queue/shape, transmit.

J3. Why cells (fixed-size) and why VOQ — what does each specifically prevent?

Cells (fixed size) → uniform fabric scheduling and even spray across fabric planes; variable-length packets would make fabric load lumpy and scheduling unfair. VOQ → eliminates head-of-line blocking: a congested egress only backs up its own queue at ingress, so traffic to uncongested egresses behind it isn't stalled. A single shared input FIFO (no VOQ) → one busy egress blocks everyone (HOL). Weak answer knows the words, misses "cells = even fabric spread, VOQ = no HOL."

J4. Shared-memory (Tomahawk) vs VOQ+HBM (Jericho) — the trade in one sentence each, and the MSD angle.

Shared-memory: one buffer pool all ports share, packet stored once + dequeued per egress by pointer → simple, shallow, lowest latency, microburst-limited. VOQ+HBM: deep buffering + no-HOL + cell fabric → absorbs sustained bursts/incast, costs ~µs of buffer latency. MSD (Max SID/label Depth) = how deep a label stack the ingress can push — caps SR-TE path length + TI-LFA repair depth; deep-stack designs need MSD ≥ (policy depth + EL/ELI + service labels).

J4b — Juniper silicon map (verified 2026-06-19, interview-critical for Juniper). Juniper builds its OWN deep-buffer/VOQ silicon — it does not use Broadcom Jericho (that's what competitors Cisco NCS5500/5700 + Arista 7280R/7500R/7800R use):

- QFX10000 (10008/10016 DC spine/core) → Q5 ASIC: custom, deep buffers, VOQ-based, queue+drop on INGRESS during congestion, no HOL — Juniper's own words; this is the Jericho-equivalent + the best deep-buffer example to cite.

- PTX (core/peering) → Express (Express 5 ~28.8 Tb/s) — competes with Jericho-based Arista R-series.

- MX (edge) → Trio (Trio 6) — VOQ + deep buffer + cell fabric.

- QFX5xxx (5100/5120/5130/5200/5700 leaf/spine) → Broadcom merchant (Trident/Tomahawk), shallow buffer.

J4c — packet flow differs by chip CLASS (Faz asked 2026-06-19): TWO flows, not three. Trident ≈ Tomahawk (same flow); Jericho is the different one. Forwarding logic identical (parse→lookup→queue→forward); buffering + fabric differ:

- Shallow (Trident/Tomahawk) = output-queued shared-memory: wire→ingress→parse+lookup→store ONCE in shared on-chip buffer→enqueue to EGRESS queue by pointer→dequeue→wire. No cells, no VOQ, no fabric spray. Lowest latency, cut-through-capable, shallow → microburst-limited. Buffer at EGRESS.

- Deep (Jericho) = ingress-VOQ + cell fabric: wire→ingress→parse+lookup→segment into fixed cells→INGRESS VOQ (per-egress, deep HBM, pointers)→egress scheduler credits/grants→cells sprayed across fabric planes→egress reassembles→wire. No HOL, deep, scales across a modular chassis fabric, ~µs latency. Buffer at INGRESS.

Room line: "forwarding logic same; buffering model is the differentiator — shallow = output-queued shared-memory (store once, no cells), deep = ingress-VOQ cell-fabric (segment, credit-schedule, reassemble); that's why one is low-latency-shallow and the other deep-buffer-no-HOL." Bonus: VOQ/cell is also what makes a modular chassis work (cells across fabric modules between line cards); a fixed shallow box is single-die, no fabric.

J5. Spine vs leaf as hardware — what does each role actually demand of the box?

Leaf (ToR): host-port density + breakout, is the VTEP (VXLAN encap/decap line-rate), EVPN feature set, moderate buffer → Trident/Tomahawk. Spine: high uplink bandwidth + wide ECMP, IP-only forwarding of the outer header (no VTEP in classic design), low jitter → Tomahawk (shallow, low-latency, AI) or Jericho (if it also does DCI/peering deep-buffer). Weak answer treats them as the same box at different sizes.

K · Multicast (market-data tie-in)

K1. Market-data is multicast; the EVPN-VXLAN fabric is unicast. How do you carry it, and what's the senior answer?

Default: keep market-data multicast in its own PIM-SSM plane, off the EVPN fabric — SSM (S,G) + IGMPv3 (known source, no RP, A/B feeds trivial), two disjoint planes. EVPN-VXLAN is unicast; don't tunnel a deterministic A/B feed through a controller-computed fabric. If forced across the SR/VXLAN core: Tree-SID (SR P2MP) or BIER (stateless). Know the boundary; don't build it.

K2. A multicast group goes dark on one switch only, others fine. Lead suspect and the tell?

IGMP snooping — lost querier → group entry ages out → that switch stops forwarding the group locally (packets arrive but aren't forwarded = snooping signature). Fix: IGMP snooping querier on the VLAN. Tell: disable snooping → floods → works, which confirms it's snooping not the network.

L2. RcvbufErrors vs InErrors — difference, and which is bigger?

Rcvbuf = socket receive buffer full; InErrors = superset (also corrupt / no-listening-port / bad-length). InErrors ≥ RcvbufErrors (a buffer-full drop charges both).

L3. ethtool -S shows rx-no-buffer climbing but the app is idle — two possible causes?

ring too small; OR softirq/CPU not draining the ring fast enough. (Not the app — idle.)

L4. softnet time_squeeze climbing — what's saturated, three fixes?

softirq is CPU-bound on that core → RSS/RPS to spread queues across cores, pin the IRQ off the app core, raise net.core.netdev_budget.

L5. Why won't a huge SO_RCVBUF save a slow consumer?

it absorbs a burst, not a sustained rate mismatch — if arrival > drain, any finite buffer fills. Buffer = shock absorber, not a fix for a slow reader.

L6. Tune this box for low latency — the checklist.

Isolation: isolcpus + nohz_full + rcu_nocbs (dedicate cores, kill the tick); SMT off. Power: governor performance, disable deep C-states, turbo on, mitigations=off (state the tradeoff). IRQ: pin NIC-queue IRQs to housekeeping cores, ban the isolated ones. NIC: ring up, coalescing down (rx-usecs 0), GRO/LRO off if latency>throughput. Steer: RSS (hw hash→queue→core) + RFS (flow→app's core = cache locality). Socket: rmem_max↑, SO_BUSY_POLL + net.core.busy_poll=50, recvmmsg to batch. Mem: mlockall, hugepages, THP off, swapoff, NIC + cores same NUMA node. (idle wake ≈ 8µs → busy-poll ≈ 4µs.)

L7. Kernel bypass — why, and Onload vs ef_vi vs DPDK?

Onload (Solarflare/AMD) = LD_PRELOAD, POSIX, no code change. ef_vi = low-level L2 API, max control. DPDK = full userspace PMD, write to its API. Cost: burns a core at 100%, no kernel TCP/firewall/tcpdump, NIC-specific. (RoCE = RDMA, bypass kernel both ends = the AI lossless transport, ties §M2.)

O1 · Jargon glossary (plain — Faz asked for every word):

  • SerDes = Serializer/Deserializer — turns the chip's parallel bits into one fast serial stream on a wire ("lane") and back. A port = several lanes.
  • NRZ vs PAM4 = bits per signal. NRZ = on/off = 1 bit. PAM4 = 4 voltage levels = 2 bits → doubles speed on the same wire. Ladder 25G NRZ → 50G PAM4 → 100G PAM4 → 200G = the SerDes generations (the engine of "each gen doubles").
  • radix = ports one chip fans out. High-radix = more ports/chip → fewer Clos tiers.
  • on-chip (SRAM) vs off-chip (HBM) = shallow buffer sits on the die (fast, ~MB); deep buffer = stacked DRAM (HBM) beside the chip (~100s MB–GB, a memory trip away).
  • VOQ = virtual output queue — per-egress queues held at INGRESS → kills head-of-line blocking.
  • cells = deep chips chop the packet into fixed-size cells, spray across fabric, reassemble at egress.
  • credit/grant = ingress VOQ must request + be granted by the egress scheduler before sending → adds latency + jitter.
  • Ramon = Broadcom FABRIC chip. Jericho = the ports; Ramon = internal switch between Jerichos across line cards = a scalable chassis.
  • DNX vs StrataXGS = just Broadcom's two product-line brand names (DNX = deep Jericho family; StrataXGS = shallow Trident/Tomahawk). Don't memorize — nobody asks the brand.

P0. What does "Elastic" actually mean across the AWS names, and name the four you meet in networking?

"Elastic" = branding for software-defined, detach/reattach, scale on demand — not a technology. The four: EC2 (Elastic Compute Cloud = the VMs); Elastic IP / EIP = a static public IPv4 you allocate and own, remappable to any instance/ENI (vs the ephemeral public IP that changes on stop/start); ENI (Elastic Network Interface) = the virtual NIC — holds the private IP(s), MAC, security groups; detachable/movable between instances; ELB (Elastic Load Balancer = ALB L7 / NLB L4 / GWLB). Trap: ElastiCache = managed Redis/Memcached, NOT networking. Weak answer treats EC2 and Elastic-IP as one word — unrelated beyond branding.

P1. Build the minimal routable VPC — the objects, and what makes a subnet "public" vs "private"?

VPC = your private network in a region, a CIDR (/16–/28). Subnets are AZ-scoped (one subnet = one Availability Zone → the resilience lever). Each subnet associates to one route table. Public subnet = its route table has 0.0.0.0/0 → an Internet Gateway (IGW); private subnet has no IGW route. Instances carry only a private IP; the public IP/EIP is 1:1 NAT'd by the IGW (the instance never sees it on its interface). Weak answer thinks "public" is a checkbox — it's purely the route table pointing at an IGW.

P2. IGW vs NAT Gateway — what each does and which way traffic flows.

IGW = bidirectional internet edge for public subnets; horizontally scaled, no cap, does the 1:1 public↔private NAT. NAT Gateway = outbound-only for private subnets (they reach out for patches etc., internet can't initiate in); AZ-scoped (one per AZ for resilience), source-NATs to an EIP, billed per-hour + per-GB. Trap: a single NAT GW = cross-AZ data charges + one AZ failure kills all private egress. Weak answer misses outbound-only + AZ-scoped.

P3. Security Group vs NACL — the senior distinction.

Security Group = stateful, on the ENI (instance level), allow-only (implicit deny), return traffic auto-allowed. NACL = stateless, at the subnet edge, ordered allow + deny by rule number, and you must explicitly allow the return (ephemeral) ports because it tracks no state. SGs = primary control; NACLs = coarse subnet backstop. Classic bug: NACL blocks ephemeral return ports → connection hangs even though the SG allows it. Weak answer can't name stateful vs stateless — that's the whole point.

P4. Two VPCs must talk — peering vs Transit Gateway, when each, and the gotcha.

VPC peering = 1:1, non-transitive (A↔B + A↔C does NOT give B↔C), no overlapping CIDRs, routes on both sides — fine for a couple. Transit Gateway (TGW) = a regional router/hub: attach many VPCs + on-prem (DX/VPN), transitive via per-attachment route tables, scales to hundreds. Gotcha: peering is an n² mesh with no transitivity → TGW is the hub-and-spoke answer. This is the box your lab's transit-VIF lands on.

P5. Hybrid — connect your DC to a VPC. Direct Connect vs VPN, and what BGP is doing (this IS your lab).

Direct Connect (DX) = a private physical link into an AWS location (1/10/100G), no internet; you cut VIFs: private VIF → VGW or TGW (VPC private space), public VIF (AWS public services), transit VIF → TGW. Site-to-Site VPN = IPsec over internet = cheap backup path. Both run eBGP: you advertise on-prem prefixes, AWS advertises the VPC CIDR; AWS terminates on a VGW (Virtual Private Gateway) or TGW. Design = DX primary + VPN backup, BGP prefers DX (local-pref/AS-path), fails to VPN. = your lab's border-leaf → VRF → simulated DX (802.1Q VIFs, transit-VIF → TGW) → "AWS" node. Weak answer knows "DX = private line" but not the VIF types or that it's plain eBGP.

P6. PrivateLink — what problem it solves that peering/TGW don't.

PrivateLink exposes a single service into your VPC as an interface endpoint = an ENI with a private IP in your subnet; consumers reach it over private space with no route exposure, no CIDR coordination, one-way (consumer→service). vs peering/TGW which join whole networks (routing + overlapping-CIDR pain). Use it to consume a SaaS / another team's service without merging networks. Weak answer conflates it with peering.

V · TCP/IP STATE + TRANSPORT INTERNALS (cold source, added 2026-07-22)

Cover the answer, say it out loud, then check. Every answer names the keeper mechanism plus what a weak answer drops.

V1. Walk the TCP connection setup and teardown as a state machine — both sides, every state, in order.

Open: client CLOSED → sends SYN → SYN-SENT; server LISTEN → receives SYN, sends SYN-ACK → SYN-RECEIVED; client receives SYN-ACK, sends ACK → ESTABLISHED; server receives ACK → ESTABLISHED.

Close (the half that gets probed): the active closer sends FIN → FIN-WAIT-1; receives ACK → FIN-WAIT-2; receives the peer's FIN, sends ACK → TIME-WAIT → after 2×MSL → CLOSED. The passive closer receives FIN, ACKs → CLOSE-WAIT; when its app closes it sends FIN → LAST-ACK → receives ACK → CLOSED.

Simultaneous close → both go FIN-WAIT-1 → CLOSING → TIME-WAIT. Weak answer stops at "SYN, SYN-ACK, ACK" and can't name CLOSE-WAIT or LAST-ACK.

V2. A box has tens of thousands of sockets in TIME-WAIT. What is that, whose problem is it, and what is the fix?

TIME-WAIT is held by the end that closed first. Duration = 2×MSL (Linux MSL 30s, so ~60s, and it is not tunable via a sysctl — tcp_fin_timeout governs FIN-WAIT-2, not this). Two jobs: absorb a lost final ACK (the peer retransmits its FIN and needs someone to answer), and drain stray old segments so a new connection on the same four-tuple can't be poisoned.

It is benign — it's connection churn, not an attack. The design fix is to make the OTHER end close first, plus connection reuse / keep-alive / pooling so you stop minting four-tuples. tcp_tw_reuse is a mitigation for outbound connections only; tcp_tw_recycle is gone from modern kernels and was NAT-hostile — never reach for it.

Weak answer: "it's a DoS" (that's SYN-RECEIVED / half-open backlog exhaustion) or "raise the timeout".

V3. Same box, tens of thousands of sockets in CLOSE-WAIT. Different diagnosis — why?

CLOSE-WAIT means the peer sent FIN and your application has not called close(). It is a bug in your code, not a kernel or network condition, and it never times out on its own — the socket sits there leaking a file descriptor until the process is restarted. TIME-WAIT = you closed and are waiting correctly; CLOSE-WAIT = you were told to close and haven't.

V4. A 100G link with 60 ms round-trip is only pulling a fraction of line rate. No loss. Why, and what do you size?

Bandwidth-delay product: 100 Gbit/s × 0.06 s = 6 Gbit = 750 MB of data in flight to keep the pipe full. TCP can only have one window in flight per RTT, so throughput = window ÷ RTT — a small window starves the pipe. Fix = window scaling (RFC 7323) plus socket buffers sized to the BDP (tcp_rmem/tcp_wmem, autotuning ceilings).

The seam to hold: this is a starvation problem at the endpoints. The router egress buffer is the opposite — an overflow problem in the network, filled by a microburst, tail-dropping. Both are sized bandwidth × delay (50 ms × 100G ≈ 625 MB of deep buffer), which is exactly why they get confused.

V5. Congestion control: what actually differs between the classic loss-based algorithms and BBR, and when does it matter?

Loss-based (Reno, CUBIC — the Linux default) treat loss as the congestion signal, so they fill the bottleneck queue until it drops — high throughput, high queueing delay (bufferbloat). BBR models the bottleneck bandwidth and minimum RTT and paces to that, so it holds the queue near-empty — lower latency, and it doesn't collapse on links with non-congestive loss (long fat / lossy paths). ECN changes the signal from a drop to a mark — the same mechanism the DC uses under DCQCN for lossless RoCE.

V6. Why is market data UDP, and what does that decision hand you and cost you?

Hands you: one-to-many (multicast has no TCP equivalent), no handshake, no head-of-line blocking, no retransmit timer sitting in the hot path. Costs you: no ordering, no delivery guarantee, no flow control, no backpressure — so the application layer rebuilds exactly the parts it needs: sequence numbers for gap detection, A/B feeds for redundancy, and a retransmit/snapshot channel off the hot path. Weak answer: "UDP is faster" without naming what the app has to re-implement.

V7. Nagle and delayed-ACK — what's the interaction, and where does it bite a trading stack?

Nagle holds a small write until the previous small segment is ACKed (to avoid tinygrams); delayed ACK holds the ACK up to ~40 ms hoping to piggyback. Together they deadlock a request/response pattern into a ~40 ms stall on small writes. Fix = TCP_NODELAY on any latency-sensitive socket (and write whole messages in one call rather than header-then-body). This is the classic "our order path has a mysterious 40 ms" answer.

V8. Path-MTU black hole — the symptom, the mechanism, the fix.

Symptom: small packets and pings fine, large transfers hang — sessions establish then stall. Mechanism: a downstream link has a smaller MTU, the sender sets DF, the router sends ICMP fragmentation-needed, and a firewall filters the ICMP — so the sender never learns and keeps retransmitting full-size segments. Detect with a large ping with DF set, walking the size down. Fix = let the ICMP through, or MSS clamping at the edge. Ties to the VXLAN case: ~50 bytes of encapsulation with a 1500-byte underlay produces exactly this.

W · ENCAPSULATION — WHAT'S ACTUALLY ON THE WIRE (cold source, added 2026-07-22)

W1. Draw the MPLS label entry, field by field, with bit widths — and say what each field is for.

32 bits total, 4 bytes: Label 20 bits · TC 3 bits (traffic class, was EXP — QoS marking) · S 1 bit (bottom-of-stack: 1 on the last label) · TTL 8 bits (copied from IP at imposition in uniform mode; decremented per hop for loop protection and for traceroute to work).

Reserved values worth knowing: 0 = IPv4 explicit-null, 2 = IPv6 explicit-null, 3 = implicit-null (the "pop, don't swap" signal that produces PHP), 7 = entropy-label indicator.

Weak answer knows "20-bit label" and drops the S-bit or thinks TTL isn't there.

W2. SR-MPLS three-segment path versus SRv6 for the same path — count the bytes and say what decides it.

SR-MPLS: 3 × 4 = 12 bytes. SRv6 classic: 40-byte IPv6 outer + 8-byte SRH base + 16 bytes per SID = 96 bytes (some stacks carry the active SID only in the destination address and omit it from the list — 80 bytes at N=3; quote the conservative figure). SRv6 uSID packs up to six 16-bit micro-SIDs into one 128-bit carrier, so short paths flatten to ~64 bytes, and the SRH can disappear entirely.

What decides it is not the nanoseconds of serialisation — it's MTU determinism on a 1500-byte WAN segment, and whether the silicon does the C-SID shift at line rate.

W3. Walk the VXLAN header stack outward from the tenant frame, and say what gives the spines their hash entropy.

[outer IP: source VTEP → destination VTEP] [UDP: destination 4789, source = hash of the inner flow] [VXLAN header: flags + 24-bit VNI] [original L2 frame] — MAC-in-UDP, ~50 bytes of overhead, so the underlay runs jumbo (9216) or you get a silent large-frame black hole.

The entropy is the outer UDP source port, computed from the inner five-tuple — without it every flow between the same VTEP pair would hash identically and polarise onto one spine. The spine routes the outer IP only and never sees a tenant MAC or the VNI.

W4. VXLAN versus GENEVE versus GRE versus MPLS-over-UDP — what problem does each newer one solve?

GRE: generic, no port-level entropy by default, no tenant ID beyond the key — pre-DC-overlay. VXLAN: adds the 24-bit VNI (16M segments versus 4K VLANs) and gets ECMP entropy free from the UDP source port. GENEVE: same shape, but TLV-extensible options — VXLAN's header is fixed, so anything a NIC/controller wants to carry (metadata, policy) has nowhere to go; NSX and offload-heavy stacks use it. MPLS-over-UDP / MPLS-over-GRE: carries a label stack across an IP-only core when you can't run MPLS end to end.

W5. The full nesting for a tenant packet crossing an EVPN-VXLAN fabric where the underlay itself is SR-MPLS DCI — name every header outward.

tenant payload → tenant L4 → tenant IP → tenant Ethernet → VXLAN + VNI → UDP → outer IP (VTEP to VTEP) → SR label stack (transport SIDs, plus entropy label indicator and entropy label if inserted) → outer Ethernet. The point of the question is whether you can keep overlay identity (VNI) separate from transport path (SID stack) — two different jobs, two different layers.

W6. Where does the entropy label sit, why does it exist, and what does it cost?

[transport label][ELI = 7][EL][service label][payload]. It exists because a transit LSR has to guess where the inner IP header is under a deep stack — and can't at all when the payload is Ethernet (L2VPN/pseudowire) or encrypted. The ingress computes a 20-bit value from the real flow; every transit hashes on that, no deep inspection. Cost = 2 labels of max-SID-depth budget, which is why an MSD of 5 on old silicon can't carry a deep SR-TE policy plus a service label plus ELI/EL.

W7. IPv6 header versus IPv4 — the three differences that change how the network behaves.

Fixed 40-byte header, no options (options moved to extension headers) — so forwarding is simpler but middleboxes often drop unknown extension headers. No router fragmentation — routers never fragment in v6, so PMTUD is mandatory and filtering ICMPv6 breaks connectivity outright. No header checksum — L2 and L4 cover it, saving a per-hop recompute. Plus no broadcast: ARP is replaced by NDP over multicast, and address assignment can be SLAAC off router advertisements.

W8. Ethernet frame: what's actually on the wire before your first byte of IP, and what's the 802.1Q tag made of?

Preamble (7) + start-of-frame delimiter (1) → destination MAC (6) + source MAC (6) → optional 802.1Q tag (4 bytes: TPID 0x8100, then PCP 3 bits + DEI 1 bit + VLAN ID 12 bits) → EtherType (2) → payload → FCS (4), then a 12-byte interframe gap. 12 bits of VLAN ID is the 4094-usable-VLAN ceiling that VXLAN's 24-bit VNI exists to break. QinQ stacks a second tag (TPID 0x88a8) — service tag outside, customer tag inside.

X2. What does "model-driven" actually buy you over scripting the CLI?

CLI scripting encodes syntax; a model encodes intent with a schema. With YANG you get a typed, validated, vendor-neutral contract: config is machine-validated before it hits a box, the same intent renders to multiple vendors, and — the part people miss — you can diff intent against operational state, because the model covers both config and state. X3. NETCONF, RESTCONF, gNMI — what's the difference and when do you reach for each?

NETCONF: XML over SSH, RPC-based, the original. Key feature is candidate config + commit + confirmed-commit with rollback — transactional change. RESTCONF: the same YANG models over HTTP/REST + JSON — simpler, web-friendly, but weaker transactional semantics. gNMI: gRPC over HTTP/2 with protobuf — binary, efficient, and the one that does streaming telemetry (Subscribe) as well as Get/Set. gNOI is the sibling for operations rather than config (reboot, cert install, file ops, ping).

Reach: gNMI where you want telemetry and scale; NETCONF where you want the transactional commit model; RESTCONF where a simple integration is enough.

X4. Streaming telemetry versus SNMP polling — why does a research/trading network care?

SNMP polls — you ask, typically every 30–60s, over UDP, per-OID, and the box burns CPU building the answer. You cannot see a microburst in a 30-second average; the counter is already smoothed. Streaming telemetry pushes on subscription — sub-second, structured, over a persistent gRPC channel, on-change or sample-interval, and it's the same YANG paths as your config so intent and state share a vocabulary.

The trading-network point: congestion that matters happens in microseconds. Anything averaged over a polling interval hides exactly the event you're hunting. This is the honest link to your buffer/microburst material.

X5. The controller dies. What happens to the network?

The right architecture answer is fail-static: the data plane keeps forwarding on the state it already has, and the controller's absence costs you change and visibility, not traffic. Anything where a controller outage drops packets is a design failure.

So you separate them: keep the distributed control plane authoritative for forwarding (BGP/OSPF/EVPN converge on their own), and let the controller/automation own intent, provisioning and telemetry. That gives a blast radius of "I can't push changes for an hour," which is survivable, instead of "the fabric is down."

Weak answer: describing a centralised controller as the forwarding brain without naming what happens when it's gone.

X6. Source of truth, drift, reconciliation — how do you keep intent and reality the same thing?

A single source of truth (the intent repo / IPAM+ DB) is authoritative; the network is a rendering of it, never the master. Then you need a reconciliation loop: continuously read operational state, diff against intent, and either alert or auto-remediate. Drift is what happens the first time someone fixes something at 3am on the CLI — and if you never diff, your model quietly becomes fiction.

The discipline that makes it real: no out-of-band changes, break-glass is logged and reconciled after, and the pipeline is the only write path.

X7. Centralised path computation — PCE/PCEP, BGP-LS, SR policy. How does the controller actually steer traffic?

BGP-LS exports the IGP link-state topology (plus SR SIDs, TE metrics, SRLGs) northbound so the controller has a full, current topology without running the IGP. The PCE computes a constrained path across it. PCEP is the session that delegates/installs that path on the headend — stateful PCE keeps ownership so it can re-optimise. The alternative south-bound is BGP SR Policy (SAFI 73), which just distributes the policy as a route — and matters to you because Arista EOS has no PCEP, so on an EOS network the answer is BGP SR Policy or controller-driven config, not PCEP.

Why SR makes this work at all: the path is a stack of SIDs imposed at the head end — there's no per-hop signalling and no state in the core, so the controller can change a path without touching every router. That's the thing RSVP-TE couldn't do at scale.

X8. If EVPN/BGP already distributes state, why would you add a controller?

Because they solve different problems. EVPN is a distributed control plane — it converges without a brain, survives partition, and scales by protocol design; that's why the DC settled on it over controller-per-fabric models. A controller earns its place at the layer above: intent, provisioning, cross-domain policy, path computation with global constraints, and telemetry aggregation — things no single router can see.

The design line to hold: distributed for forwarding, centralised for intent. Centralising forwarding decisions re-creates a single point of failure that the routing protocols spent thirty years eliminating.

Raising trust-bootstrap unprompted is what separates an architect answer from an ops answer.

X10. Build versus buy — how did you decide, and how would you decide again?

The axes you actually weigh: fit to the operational model (does the product's opinion match how we run?), lock-in and the exit cost, multi-vendor reality (an off-the-shelf controller that only speaks one vendor is a strategy, not a tool), the feature-request latency — how long from "we need X" to having X — versus the cost of owning code: staffing, on-call, and the fact that you now maintain it forever.

At Sky, in-house end-to-end orchestration won — the estate and operating model didn't fit a product's opinion, and the integration surface was the whole point. The honest counterweight to state out loud: build means you own it forever, and that's a staffing commitment, not a project.

X11. How do you migrate a live network onto an automated pipeline without an outage?

Read before write. Phase 1 the pipeline only reads and diffs — you build confidence that the model matches reality with zero risk. Phase 2 it writes one low-risk element (banners, SNMP, NTP) across the estate to prove the mechanics. Phase 3 it owns a full device class, starting at the edge, not the core. Throughout: canary → staged rings → full, with automated pre- and post-checks on every change and a rollback that's been tested, not theorised.

The bit interviewers listen for: you designed the transition and you were there when it went wrong. Name a concrete failure and what the fix taught you — that's the credibility, not the happy path.

X12. Automation lets you break 5,000 devices in one second. How do you bound that?

Blast radius is a design parameter. Concretely: dry-run/diff before apply; change budgets (no more than N devices or one failure domain per window); canary then rings; automated health gates between rings so the pipeline stops itself; confirmed-commit / commit-with-rollback timers (NETCONF gives you this natively) so a box that loses management reverts on its own; and guardrails in the model — policy that refuses a change that would, say, drain both spines.

The one-liner: "The scary thing about automation isn't that it makes mistakes — it's that it makes them uniformly, everywhere, instantly. So the pipeline has to be more conservative than a human would be."

Y · PCAP READING (in scope, 27/28 Jul — cold source, added 2026-07-22)

They may put a capture in front of you. The differentiator is not Wireshark button knowledge — it's knowing where you captured, what the capture is lying about, and what the evidence rules out.

Y1. Before you read a single packet: where did you capture, and what did that cost you?

Optical/passive tap — sees everything including errors, doesn't drop under load, no device CPU involved. SPAN/mirror — convenient, but it drops silently when the mirror oversubscribes the destination port, is deprioritised against real traffic, and strips or hides L1/L2 errors (CRC, runts). So "the capture shows loss" on a SPAN may mean the SPAN dropped it, not the network. Host capture — sees the traffic after NIC offload and before the app, so it proves what the kernel received, not what was on the wire.

Always say "and I'd confirm the capture point isn't the source of the symptom" — that single sentence is what a senior answer sounds like.

Y2. The capture shows 64KB TCP segments on a 1500-MTU link. Is the network broken?

No — that's offload lying to you. TSO/GSO (transmit) and LRO/GRO (receive) mean the host hands the NIC one giant buffer and the NIC segments it, so a host-side capture shows a super-sized pseudo-packet that never existed on the wire. Same family: checksum offload makes outbound checksums look "bad" in a local capture because they haven't been computed yet.

The fix when it matters: capture on a tap, or disable offloads (ethtool -K) for the test. Knowing this stops you chasing a phantom MTU problem.

Y3. Produce these tcpdump filters cold.

Write to disk, don't print: tcpdump -i eth0 -w cap.pcap -s0 (-s0 = full packet; a short snaplen silently truncates). Don't resolve names in the hot path: -nn. Ring buffer for a long hunt: -W 10 -C 100 .

Host/port: host 10.1.1.1 and port 179. Subnet: net 10.1.0.0/16. Multicast group: host 239.1.1.1. Only SYNs: 'tcp[tcpflags] & tcp-syn != 0'. RSTs: 'tcp[tcpflags] & tcp-rst != 0'. VLAN-tagged: vlan and host x. VXLAN: udp port 4789. RoCEv2: udp port 4791.

Y4. In the capture: retransmission, out-of-order, or duplicate? How do you tell them apart, and what does each imply?

Look at sequence number versus arrival time and IP ID. Retransmission — same sequence, arriving later than the gap, typically after an RTO or after 3 dup-ACKs. Out-of-order — a lower sequence arriving between higher ones, with a tiny time delta (it took a different path / got reordered in the fabric), and no loss actually occurred. Duplicate — the same segment twice with no gap to fill (often a mirroring artefact — you captured the same packet on two ports).

Implication matters: retransmissions = real loss (or a lost ACK); out-of-order = load-balancing / ECMP polarisation or a parallel-path asymmetry, which is a design finding, not a loss finding; duplicates = suspect your capture setup first.

Y5. "TCP Window Full" and "TCP ZeroWindow" — who's at fault?

Zero window = the receiver's problem. Its application isn't draining the socket buffer, so it advertises zero and tells the sender to stop; the sender then window-probes. This is a slow consumer — a stalled app, a blocked thread, an undersized SO_RCVBUF — not a network fault. Window Full is the sender having filled the receiver's advertised window and being unable to send more until an ACK frees space.

The line: "zero-window means the network delivered fine and the application couldn't keep up." Contrast with RcvbufErrors on the UDP side, which is the same disease with no backpressure — the kernel just drops.

Y6. Duplicate ACKs — what's happening and what does the count tell you?

A receiver that gets an out-of-order segment re-ACKs the last in-order byte — that's a dup-ACK. Three dup-ACKs trigger fast retransmit (the sender resends without waiting for the RTO) and fast recovery. SACK blocks in the options tell you exactly which ranges arrived, so the sender resends only the hole rather than everything from the gap.

Reading it: lots of dup-ACKs + fast retransmits = moderate loss, recovering well. Loss with RTO timeouts instead (a long gap, then a retransmit, then the window collapses) = far worse — that's where throughput falls off a cliff.

Y7. You see a RST. Give the causes.

(1) Nothing listening on the port — a connection refused. (2) The application closed abortively (SO_LINGER 0) instead of a graceful FIN — common in trading stacks that want the socket gone now. (3) A firewall or middlebox injecting a RST to tear the session down — the tell is a TTL or IP-ID that doesn't match the real peer's other packets. (4) Traffic arriving for a connection the peer has no state for — after a reboot, a failover, or a NAT/state-table timeout, which is the classic "idle session dies after N minutes" case.

The middlebox-injection tell (mismatched TTL) is the one that impresses.

Y8. Request/response with a repeating ~40 ms stall. Diagnose from the capture.

Nagle interacting with delayed ACK. The sender has a small write outstanding and Nagle won't release the next one until the previous is ACKed; the receiver's delayed-ACK timer sits on the ACK for up to ~40 ms hoping to piggyback it on data. Neither side is broken; together they deadlock into a quantised ~40 ms.

In the capture it's unmistakable: the delay is suspiciously constant, it only hits small writes, and it disappears on large ones. Fix: TCP_NODELAY, and write the whole message in a single call rather than header-then-body.

Y9. Session establishes, then large transfers hang. What do you look for?

PMTU black hole. In the capture: the handshake and small packets are fine, then full-size segments with DF set are sent repeatedly and never ACKed, and no ICMP fragmentation-needed comes back — because a firewall ate it. Confirm with a large ping with DF, walking the size down to find the real MTU.

Fix: permit ICMP type 3 code 4, or MSS-clamp at the edge. The DC version of this is a VXLAN/VPN overlay adding ~50 bytes on a 1500-byte underlay — same signature, and it's why overlays want a 9000-byte underlay.

Y10. A multicast receiver says it's getting nothing. Prove where the tree is broken, from captures.

Work the path in order and say which capture answers which question: (1) At the receiver: is there an IGMP membership report leaving it, and for IGMPv3 is it an INCLUDE (S,G) or a v2-style (*,G)? A receiver that never joined is an app/socket problem, not a network one. (2) On the access switch: is IGMP snooping programming the port, and is there a querier on the VLAN? No querier means snooping tables age out and traffic dies after a couple of minutes — the classic intermittent multicast fault. (3) At the first-hop router: PIM messages — with ASM you should see the **(*,G) join toward the RP and then the (S,G) switchover to the SPT; with SSM there's no RP and no shared tree, you go straight to (S,G). (4) Anywhere on the path: RPF** — the source must be reachable out the interface the traffic arrived on, or it's dropped silently. Asymmetric routing kills multicast for this reason alone.

The market-data framing: SSM removes the RP, the shared tree, and the switchover entirely — no RP failure mode, deterministic SPT from the first packet. That's why finance and IPTV both land on it.

Y11. A trading desk reports missing market data. Was it the network or the application?

The feed's own sequence numbers are the evidence — the app reports a gap at sequence N. Then you prove where it went: if the capture at the host NIC contains sequence N, the network delivered it and the host or app dropped it — check netstat -su / nstat RcvbufErrors and socket buffer sizing, which is the same story as Y5 without backpressure. If the capture doesn't contain it, walk upstream to the next tap.

Also: A/B feeds are two independent paths carrying the same sequence space — if the gap is on A only, it's a path problem and arbitration should have covered it; if it's on A and B at the same sequence, it's at the source or a shared segment. That A-versus-B comparison is the diagnosis, and it's the answer that shows you've actually worked a market-data net.

Y12. Interface utilisation reads 40% but you're dropping. Explain, and prove it from a capture.

Microburst. Utilisation counters are averages over the polling interval — 40% over 30 seconds hides a burst that hit line rate for 200 µs and overflowed the egress buffer. Ports are drop-free at the average and lossy at the instant.

Prove it: Wireshark IO graph at 1 ms (or finer) resolution on a capture with hardware timestamps, and you'll see the burst; or read the box's queue-depth/drop counters rather than utilisation. Cause is usually incast — many senders synchronising onto one receiver — or a speed mismatch (100G into 10G). Fix is buffer, pacing, or ECN/DCQCN, not "add bandwidth."

This is the single most common "the network is fine, look at the graph" argument you'll be asked to demolish.

Y13. How much do you trust the timestamps?

Software timestamps are applied when the kernel sees the packet — subject to interrupt coalescing, scheduling and NAPI batching, which is tens of microseconds of noise and not good enough for latency work. Hardware timestamping on the NIC/tap is what you use when the number matters. For comparing captures at two points, both must share a clock discipline — PTP (with hardware timestamping, ideally boundary clocks), not NTP.

Say this unprompted when asked to measure latency from captures: "first I'd establish whether the timestamps are hardware and whether the two capture points are PTP-disciplined, or the delta is meaningless."

Y14. An overlay/RoCE capture — what are you decoding and what do you look for?

VXLAN: outer MAC/IP/UDP with dest port 4789, then the 8-byte VXLAN header (VNI), then the original L2 frame. Two things to check: the outer source port, which is a hash of the inner flow and is the only entropy ECMP has — if it's constant, all your overlay traffic is pinned to one path; and MTU, per Y9.

RoCEv2: UDP port 4791, InfiniBand transport headers over UDP. What you look for is the lossless machinery: PFC pause frames (802.1Qbb) and ECN marks with CNPs (congestion notification packets) coming back under DCQCN. Lots of CNPs = congestion being managed; lots of PFC pause = you've pushed it to the blunt instrument, with head-of-line blocking and possible PFC storm / deadlock risk. The design point: ECN/DCQCN should be doing the work; PFC is the last resort.

Z · BGP — QUIZ-ROUND DEPTH (cold source, added 2026-07-22)

Section B covers the DC underlay only. This is the general BGP a hedge-fund panel probes: path selection, policy, scale, convergence, and the failure modes.

Z1. Give the best-path selection order, in order, and say where the ties actually get broken in practice.

1 Highest weight (Cisco-local, never leaves the box). 2 Highest LOCAL_PREF (iBGP-wide — how you choose an exit). 3 Locally originated (network/aggregate/redistributed beats learned). 4 Shortest AS_PATH. 5 Lowest origin (IGP < EGP < Incomplete). 6 Lowest MED (only compared between paths from the same neighbouring AS, unless always-compare-med). 7 eBGP over iBGP. 8 Lowest IGP metric to the next-hop ("hot-potato" — dump it out the nearest door). 9 Oldest eBGP path (stability tie-break). 10 Lowest router-ID. 11 Shortest cluster-list. 12 Lowest neighbour address.

In practice almost everything is decided by steps 2, 4 and 8 — LOCAL_PREF for policy, AS_PATH when you have no policy, IGP metric for hot-potato. Quoting the list is table stakes; the senior move is naming which knob you'd actually turn and why: LOCAL_PREF is inbound-traffic-into-your-AS control, and it's the blunt one because it beats AS_PATH.

Z2. LOCAL_PREF, MED, AS-path prepend — which direction does each steer, and which is reliable?

LOCAL_PREF — iBGP-only (never crosses an eBGP boundary), sets which exit your AS uses for outbound traffic. Fully under your control, therefore reliable. MED — sent to a neighbour AS to hint which of your entry points they should use for inbound traffic. Only compared between paths from the same AS, and your neighbour can and usually does ignore it. AS-path prepend — makes a path look longer to influence inbound traffic, but it's beaten by anyone else's LOCAL_PREF upstream, so it's a suggestion, not control.

The line to hold: you control your outbound traffic; you can only influence your inbound. Everything else — prepending, MED, selective advertisement, communities to your upstream — is negotiation with someone else's policy.

Z3. Standard, extended, and large communities — what's the difference and how do you actually use them?

Standard = 32 bits, conventionally ASN:value — breaks for 4-byte ASNs, since the ASN no longer fits in 16 bits. Large (RFC 8092) = 96 bits, three 32-bit fields (ASN:function:parameter) — the modern answer, built precisely for 4-byte ASNs. Extended = 64 bits, typed — this is what carries Route Targets in L3VPN/EVPN, not general policy tagging.

The operational pattern: tag richly at ingress, act at egress. The edge stamps a community describing what a route is and where it came from (customer/peer/transit, region, site); every downstream policy then matches on the community rather than on prefixes. That's what makes policy auditable and lets you change behaviour estate-wide without touching prefix lists.

Z4. iBGP requires a full mesh. Why, and what are the two ways out — with the cost of each?

Why: iBGP has no loop-prevention attribute — the AS_PATH isn't prepended within an AS — so the rule is a route learned from an iBGP peer is never re-advertised to another iBGP peer. That forces n(n−1)/2 sessions.

Route reflectors — the RR is allowed to re-advertise, with loop prevention moved to ORIGINATOR_ID (drop it if it's mine) and CLUSTER_LIST (drop it if my cluster-id is already in it). Cost: the RR reflects only its own best path, so clients lose path diversity — which is why you need ADD-PATH to restore multipath and fast reroute, and why RR placement must reflect the physical topology or you get suboptimal hot-potato routing.

Confederations — split the AS into sub-ASes that run eBGP between them. Cost: complexity, and it's mostly historical; RRs won.

Z5. Multipath — why doesn't it just work, and what do you need to turn on?

BGP installs one best path by default. For eBGP multipath the candidate paths must be equal through the whole decision process up to the tie-break, and critically the AS_PATH must be identical — same length and same content. In a CLOS that's why spines share an ASN; if every spine had a unique ASN the AS_PATHs would differ and you'd get one path, not N.

Knobs: maximum-paths N, plus bestpath as-path multipath-relax to accept equal-length-but-different AS_PATHs. iBGP multipath additionally wants an equal IGP metric to the next-hops. Weak answer: "enable ECMP" without naming the AS_PATH condition.

Z6. You need unequal-cost load sharing across WAN links of different capacity. How, without MPLS-TE?

BGP DMZ Link Bandwidth (an extended community carrying the link's bandwidth). Paths are installed with weights proportional to bandwidth, so a 100G and a 10G path share traffic 10:1 instead of 1:1. Requires multipath enabled, and the community must be propagated where it needs to act.

Why this matters as an argument: UCMP is often given as a reason to deploy SR. It isn't — plain IP/BGP already does unequal-cost sharing this way. The honest case for SR is explicit path control, TI-LFA, and inter-domain source routing, not load balancing. Being the person who volunteers the counter-example is worth more than reciting SR benefits.

Z7. BGP convergence is slow. Why, and what makes it fast?

Why: it's TCP-based and timer-driven (default keepalive 60 / hold 180), it batches updates (MRAI / advertisement-interval), and after a failure the router must re-run best-path across potentially millions of prefixes and reprogram the FIB — that's the real delay at scale, not the protocol chatter.

Fast: BFD to detect the failure in milliseconds instead of waiting on hold time; fast-external-fallover so a link-down kills the session immediately; next-hop tracking so an IGP change invalidates paths without waiting for BGP; and PIC (Prefix Independent Convergence) — pre-install a backup next-hop in the FIB so failover is a single pointer swap independent of prefix count. PIC is the answer that shows you understand the real bottleneck is FIB reprogramming.

Z8. Route flap damping — do you use it?

No — and know why. Classic damping (RFC 2439) suppresses a flapping prefix with an exponentially-decaying penalty, but RIPE-378 showed it badly over-penalises: because a single flap at the edge produces multiple updates as it propagates through path exploration, damping suppresses stable prefixes for far longer than intended, making outages worse. The industry stopped using it, or moved to RFC 7196 "damping-lite" with much higher thresholds.

The senior answer: "Damping is largely deprecated — the fix for instability is BFD plus fixing the flapping link, not punishing the prefix."

Z9. Graceful Restart, NSR, and BGP add-path — what problem does each solve?

GR — the control plane restarts but the forwarding plane keeps forwarding; the peer acts as a helper, holding routes as stale for a grace period instead of withdrawing them. Requires the peer's cooperation, and it's a lie if the box really did lose forwarding state. NSR/NSF with SSO — the box keeps BGP state on a standby RE/supervisor, so the peer never even notices; no helper needed, costs you hardware. ADD-PATH (RFC 7911) — lets a peer advertise multiple paths for the same prefix instead of only its best; this is what restores diversity behind a route reflector, enabling multipath and PIC backup paths at the edge.

Z10. How do you secure a BGP session, and what actually gets used?

GTSM / TTL security (RFC 5082) — send with TTL 255 and require ≥254 on receipt, so a spoofer more than one hop away can't reach the session at all. Cheap and effective. TCP-MD5 (legacy) or TCP-AO (RFC 5925, the modern replacement) for authentication. Max-prefix limits with a warning threshold so a misconfigured peer can't blow up your RIB — this is the one that saves you in practice. Prefix filters and AS-path filters in both directions, with the default being deny.

For the internet edge: RPKI origin validation (is this AS entitled to originate this prefix?) — the deployed piece of BGP security; BGPsec (path validation) is essentially not deployed.

Z11. RD versus RT — people mix these up. Be precise.

Route Distinguisher makes a prefix unique — it's prepended to the IPv4 prefix to build a VPNv4 NLRI so two customers' overlapping 10.0.0.0/8s are distinguishable in one BGP table. It carries no policy meaning whatsoever. Route Target is an extended community that controls membership — which VRFs import and export the route. RD = uniqueness, RT = policy. Hub-and-spoke, extranet and route leaking are all built by asymmetric RT import/export, never by the RD.

Z12. BGP-LS — what is it and why would you run it?

It carries the IGP link-state database northbound over BGP — nodes, links, prefixes, TE attributes, SRLGs, and SR SIDs — so a controller/PCE gets a complete, live topology without participating in the IGP. It's a reporting protocol: you don't route on it. Pairs with BGP SR Policy or PCEP to push computed paths back down. This is the join between your BGP and your SDN answers.

Z13. MED oscillation / route oscillation with route reflectors — what's the failure?

With RRs, a reflector only knows its own best path, so different RRs can make different decisions on partial information, and with MED comparison (which is non-transitive — only comparable within the same neighbour AS) the decision process can be non-deterministic and oscillate persistently (RFC 3345). Symptoms: routes flapping with no link flapping.

Mitigations: always-compare-med or deterministic-med applied consistently estate-wide, ADD-PATH so reflectors see diversity, and sane RR placement/cluster design. The root cause worth naming: partial visibility plus a non-transitive comparison rule.

Z14. Design it: a fund with research DCs, trading colos, and exchange connections. How do you lay out BGP?

Keep the failure domains explicit. Typically: each site is its own AS (or an AS per function), eBGP between sites so policy is enforced at every boundary and blast radius is bounded, iBGP with RRs inside each site. Exchange/venue connections terminate on dedicated edge routers with strict inbound filtering and max-prefix — you accept exactly what you expect and nothing else, because a venue leaking a full table into your fabric is an outage.

Say the separation out loud: market-data and order paths are latency-critical and deliberately simple; the research/inter-DC WAN is where you can afford richer policy and engineering. Design decisions that increase convergence complexity go on the research side, never the trade path.

Z15. Where does BGP actually not belong in a trading network?

On the µs-critical exchange-to-colo path. Those are short, fixed, often layer-2 or single-hop, and what you want is determinism and the absolute minimum of state and reconvergence, not policy richness. Adding a protocol that converges in seconds to a path measured in microseconds buys you nothing and adds a failure mode.

Same reasoning as keeping market-data multicast on its own PIM-SSM plane. The general principle a panel is listening for: complexity belongs where the value is (the research/WAN side), and is designed out of the fast path.

Z16. A prefix isn't being advertised. Walk your troubleshooting.

In order, and state what each step rules out: (1) Is it in the local RIB/BGP table at all — is it being originated (network statement, redistribution, aggregate) or learned? (2) Is it the best path? BGP only advertises its best path — if it lost selection you'll never see it leave. (3) Is the next-hop reachable and resolvable? An unresolvable next-hop makes a path invalid and unadvertised — the classic missing next-hop-self on an RR/iBGP edge. (4) Outbound policy — route-map/prefix-list/community filter dropping it. (5) The iBGP re-advertisement rule — learned from an iBGP peer and you're not a reflector, so it's not passed on. (6) Peer-side inbound policy or a max-prefix that has shut the session or is discarding.

Naming step 3 and step 5 early is the tell of someone who has actually done this at 3am.

AA · OSPF — QUIZ-ROUND DEPTH (cold source, added 2026-07-22)

AA1. LSA types — list them and say what each one is for.

1 Router — every router, per area: its links and costs. 2 Network — generated by the DR on a multi-access segment, lists who's attached. 3 Summary (inter-area) — from the ABR, describes a prefix in another area. 4 ASBR-summary — from the ABR, tells the area how to reach the ASBR. 5 External — from the ASBR, redistributed routes, flooded AS-wide. 7 NSSA-external — an external injected into an NSSA, which cannot carry type 5; the ABR translates 7→5 on the way out. 9/10/11 Opaque — link-local / area / AS scope; type 10 is the one that matters to you, it carries TE attributes and Segment Routing (prefix-SIDs, adjacency-SIDs, SRGB).

Weak answer stops at 1/2/3/5 and can't say what a type 4 is for.

AA2. Area types — what does each block, and why would you use it?

Backbone (area 0) — everything must touch it. Standard — all LSA types. Stub — blocks type 5 (no externals); ABR injects a default. Totally stubby (Cisco) — blocks 3 and 5, default only: the smallest possible table, and the right answer for a leaf site that has one way out. NSSA — a stub that does have its own ASBR, so it allows type 7 locally and translates to 5 at the ABR. Totally NSSA — NSSA plus blocking type 3.

The design point: area type is a blast-radius and table-size decision. A site with a single exit gains nothing from carrying the full topology and loses reconvergence time carrying it.

AA3. Walk the neighbour state machine and say what each transition proves.

Down → Init (I've heard their hello but I'm not in it — one-way) → 2-Way (I see myself in their hello — bidirectional confirmed; DR/BDR election happens here) → ExStart (master/slave negotiation and initial DBD sequence) → Exchange (DBD headers describing each database) → Loading (LSRs for what I'm missing, LSUs in reply) → Full (databases synchronised).

Also Attempt — NBMA only, where hellos are unicast to a configured neighbour.

Two DROTHERs on a broadcast segment deliberately stay at 2-Way and that is not a fault — they only go Full with the DR and BDR. Being able to say that unprompted is the differentiator.

AA4. Stuck in ExStart/Exchange. What is it, almost always?

MTU mismatch. DBD exchange carries the interface MTU and the receiver rejects a DBD advertising an MTU larger than its own, so the two bounce between ExStart and Exchange forever. Fix the MTU (or ip ospf mtu-ignore, which is papering over it).

The other candidates, in order: unicast/duplex or ACL problems blocking the higher-numbered packet types while hellos still get through, and a duplicate router-ID. Naming MTU first is the expected answer.

AA5. Stuck in Init or never forming — checklist.

Hellos are being sent but adjacency won't build: mismatched hello/dead interval, mismatched area ID, mismatched area type flag (stub/NSSA bit — a stub and a non-stub will not become neighbours), authentication mismatch, mismatched subnet/mask on the segment, mismatched network type (p2p one side, broadcast the other), or passive-interface configured. If you're stuck at Init specifically, it's one-way — they're not hearing you, so suspect a unidirectional link or an inbound filter/ACL on their side.

AA6. DR/BDR — why does it exist, how is it elected, and what's the gotcha?

On a multi-access segment, full-mesh adjacency is n(n−1)/2 and the flooding is quadratic. DR/BDR reduces it to a star: everyone goes Full with DR and BDR only, flooding to 224.0.0.6 (DR/BDR) and the DR flooding back to 224.0.0.5.

Elected by highest priority, tie-break highest router-ID. Gotchas: the election is non-preemptive — a better router arriving later does not take over, so the DR is often whoever booted first, and forcing it means clearing the process. Priority 0 means never eligible. And in a modern fabric the correct answer is usually to avoid the whole mechanism by making point-to-point links ip ospf network point-to-point, which also skips type-2 LSAs and speeds convergence.

AA7. Route preference within OSPF, and how does an external route get its cost?

Intra-area (O) > Inter-area (O IA) > E1 > E2 (and N1/N2 for NSSA). E2 is the default: the cost is the external metric only, ignoring the internal path cost — so all routers see the same cost regardless of distance to the ASBR (tie-break on forwarding cost). E1 adds the internal cost to the ASBR, so it reflects real topology. Use E1 when you have multiple ASBRs and want the nearest one chosen; E2 is fine when there's one exit.

AA8. Summarisation — where can you do it and where can't you?

Only at the ABR (area range — summarising type 3s between areas) and at the ASBR (summary-address — summarising externals as they're injected). You cannot summarise within an area, because every router in an area must build an identical SPF tree from an identical LSDB — summarising inside would mean lying to the SPF calculation.

That constraint is the argument for multi-area design: areas are the only place OSPF lets you hide topology, and hiding topology is how you bound both LSDB size and the number of routers that must re-run SPF for a remote flap.

AA9. How big should an area be, and what actually limits it?

Don't quote a number as doctrine — name the four real limits: (1) LSDB size and flooding volume in the area; (2) SPF run time, which scales with nodes and links; (3) how many routers must re-run SPF for any one link flap — that's the blast radius; (4) router CPU/memory, the weakest box in the area sets the ceiling.

The engineering answer: keep flapping edges out of area 0, put stable high-capacity core in the backbone, and use stub/totally-stubby at the leaves so a leaf flap doesn't ripple. If asked for a figure, give it as "a few hundred routers with modern CPUs, but the topology's stability matters far more than the count."

AA10. Virtual links — what are they and what's your position?

A tunnel through a transit area to connect an area that has no physical adjacency to area 0 (or to repair a partitioned backbone). Your position should be: they're a migration or emergency tool, not a design. They add a dependency on the transit area's stability, they're easy to forget, and they usually mean the area design is wrong. The correct fix is normally to re-home the area onto the backbone or to use a proper tunnel/GRE if you genuinely can't.

AA11. OSPF versus IS-IS — why do carriers and large fabrics often pick IS-IS?

IS-IS runs directly on layer 2 (CLNS), not on IP — so the routing protocol isn't reachable via the data plane it's building, which is a genuine security and robustness property. It's TLV-based, so extending it (IPv6, multi-topology, SR) is adding a TLV rather than defining new LSA types — which is why SR landed cleanly in IS-IS first. It has two levels rather than OSPF's area-type zoo, and its area boundary is on the link, not on the router, which makes multi-area design simpler. Scaling behaviour with large LSPs is generally kinder.

OSPF's counter-case: far more widely understood, better multi-vendor enterprise support, and OSPFv3/opaque LSAs cover the same SR ground now. For HRT's plain-IP OSPF network the honest answer is "OSPF is already there and works; changing IGP is not a benefit on its own."

AA12. OSPF fast convergence — what do you actually tune?

Detection: BFD (milliseconds) instead of fast-hellos — cheaper on CPU and vendor-consistent. Reaction: SPF throttling (spf-timers — initial delay, hold, max) and LSA throttling/arrival timers, tuned down from the conservative defaults but with exponential backoff so a flapping link can't melt the CPU. Repair: LFA / remote-LFA, and with SR, TI-LFA which gives a guaranteed post-convergence backup for any topology using a SID stack — that's the one worth naming because classic LFA has coverage gaps in rings. Prefix prioritisation so loopbacks (and therefore BGP next-hops) get installed first.

The ordering matters: detect fast, react in a damped way, and pre-compute the repair. Turning timers down without backoff is how you cause the outage.

AA13. How does Segment Routing get into OSPF?

Via opaque LSAs (type 10, area scope): the Extended Prefix LSA carries Prefix-SIDs (typically an index against the SRGB, so it's globally meaningful with a locally-allocated label range) and the Extended Link LSA carries Adjacency-SIDs (locally significant, per-link). The SR Capabilities/SRGB are advertised in the Router Information LSA. Being index-based rather than absolute-label-based is the key design point — it's why SRGB ranges don't have to be identical estate-wide, though keeping them identical is what everyone actually does for sanity.

The turn-on story: SR is additive to a running OSPF — advertise SRGB, assign prefix-SIDs to loopbacks, and the labels appear; no new protocol, no LDP.

AA14. Design: OSPF for a research/inter-DC network spanning several sites. Lay it out.

Backbone across the WAN core, an area per site, ABRs at the site edge, summarise at the ABR so an intra-site flap doesn't propagate, and stub/NSSA at leaf sites with a single exit. Keep the loopback addressing summarisable per site — the addressing plan is what makes the area design possible, and doing this backwards is the most common real-world failure.

Then say what it buys: bounded SPF blast radius per site, a small LSDB at the edge, and a stable backbone — and, if SR is coming, the SRGB and prefix-SID plan follows the same per-site block structure.

AA15. A link flaps in a remote area. Who recalculates what?

Inside that area: full SPF on every router (type 1/2 change). At the ABR: it regenerates affected type 3s only if the reachability or cost changes — the topology detail never leaves the area. In other areas: routers run a partial SPF (a type 3 is a distance-vector-like leaf update, not a topology change), and in a totally stubby area they see nothing at all because they only hold a default.

That single walk-through is the argument for areas, and it's a much better answer than "areas reduce the routing table."

AB · MPLS + SR — QUIZ-ROUND DEPTH (cold source, added 2026-07-22)

Your strong ground. The risk here isn't knowing it — it's giving a 2010 answer. Every question should land on "and this is why SR replaced it."

AB1. Label operations and the router roles — be precise about who does what.

Ingress PE (headend) does push (imposition) after an IP lookup. P routers do swap — a single label lookup, no IP lookup, which was the original point. Egress does pop (disposition) and then the IP/VPN lookup. PHP (penultimate-hop popping): the egress signals implicit-null (label 3) so the second-to-last router pops the transport label, sparing the egress a double lookup (pop then IP). Use explicit-null (0/2) instead when you need to preserve the TC/QoS bits to the last hop.

AB2. How do labels get distributed? Name every mechanism and what it's for.

LDP — hop-by-hop, follows the IGP, zero traffic engineering; simple and ubiquitous. RSVP-TE — signalled, constraint-based, reserves bandwidth, does FRR. BGP-LU (RFC 8277/3107) — labels carried in BGP, used to stitch inter-AS and seamless-MPLS domains. Segment Routing — no label-distribution protocol at all: the IGP floods SIDs as an attribute, and the path is a stack imposed at the head end.

The thesis sentence: SR removed an entire protocol from the network. LDP and RSVP-TE both exist only to build per-LSP state; SR encodes the path in the packet instead.

AB3. LDP-IGP synchronisation — what's the failure it prevents?

A link comes back. The IGP converges in milliseconds and starts putting traffic on it, but LDP hasn't finished exchanging bindings, so there's no label — the LSP is broken and traffic is black-holed until LDP catches up. LDP-IGP sync makes the IGP advertise the link at max metric until LDP signals it's ready.

This is the canonical "why LDP is fragile" story, and it's a good one to volunteer: SR has no equivalent failure, because there is no second protocol to be out of step with the IGP.

AB4. MPLS L3VPN — walk the full path of a packet with the label stack.

CE sends IP to PE. PE looks up in the VRF (selected by incoming interface), finds a VPNv4 route learned by MP-BGP from the remote PE, and pushes two labels: the VPN/service label (inner, allocated by the remote PE, identifies the VRF/prefix) and the transport label (outer, gets it across the core to that PE). P routers only ever look at the outer label — they have no VPN knowledge at all, which is the whole scaling argument. At the penultimate hop the transport label is popped (PHP); the egress PE sees the VPN label, maps it to the VRF, and does the IP lookup there.

RD makes the prefix unique in BGP; RT controls which VRFs import it. The core carries zero customer state — that separation is the product.

AB5. RSVP-TE — how it works and why it didn't survive.

Headend computes a path (CSPF over the TE database), signals it downstream with PATH, the tail returns RESV with the label, and every router along the way holds state for that LSP and refreshes it. Gives you real bandwidth admission control and FRR.

Why it lost: state is O(LSPs) at every midpoint, in a full mesh that's O(n²) tunnels; soft-state refresh burns CPU; adding a node means touching the mesh; and it's ECMP-hostile (an LSP is one path, so you fight the fabric's natural load spreading). SR gives the same explicit-path control with state only in the packet and in the head end, and it's ECMP-native.

AB6. MPLS FRR versus TI-LFA — what changed?

RSVP FRR pre-signals a bypass (link/node protection, one tunnel protecting many LSPs) or detour (per-LSP) so the PLR switches in ~50 ms — but you must pre-build and maintain those protection tunnels.

TI-LFA computes the post-convergence path — the path traffic will take anyway after the IGP reconverges — and encodes it as a SID stack, so there's no tunnel to pre-signal and no transient microloop. Coverage is 100% for any topology, where classic LFA has gaps (rings especially) and remote-LFA needs a targeted LDP session to a PQ node. The one-liner: TI-LFA repairs to where the network is going, not to a tunnel you built in advance.

AB7. Segment Routing — the SID types and what each one is.

Prefix-SID — global, advertised as an index into the SRGB, identifies a node/prefix; forwarded ECMP-aware along the IGP shortest path. Node-SID — a prefix-SID on a loopback (the common case). Adjacency-SID — local to a router, identifies one specific link; this is what lets you pin a path away from the shortest path. Binding-SID — a single SID that expands to a whole stack or policy at the router that owns it: it compresses the stack (MSD relief) and is the mechanism for inter-domain stitching without either domain knowing the other's SIDs. Anycast-SID — the same prefix-SID on multiple nodes, for plane/set selection.

The design consequence: shortest path costs one SID; explicit path costs one SID per deviation. You only pay for the constraint you actually need.

AB8. SRGB — what is it and what happens if it's inconsistent?

The Segment Routing Global Block is the local label range reserved for global SIDs (commonly 16000–23999). Prefix-SIDs are advertised as an index, and each router computes the local label as SRGB base + index — which is precisely why the SRGB can differ per node and still work.

But in practice everyone keeps it identical estate-wide, because a uniform SRGB means the same prefix has the same label everywhere, which makes troubleshooting, traceroute output and packet captures readable. Inconsistent SRGBs aren't broken; they're an operational tax you pay every time you debug.

AB9. MSD — why does it constrain your design?

Maximum SID Depth is how many labels the hardware can impose. Older merchant silicon manages 3–5; that's a hard ceiling on how explicit a path you can express, because each deviation from shortest-path costs a SID.

Two ways out: binding-SIDs to compress a long stack into one, and anycast/loose paths — express the path as a few waypoints rather than hop-by-hop. This is a real constraint to raise unprompted in a design round, because it's where SR-TE theory meets the ASIC. It also cuts the other way for SRv6, where the SRH is the equivalent cost in bytes rather than label slots.

AB10. SR-MPLS versus SRv6 — make the actual argument.

SR-MPLS: reuses the MPLS data plane, 4 bytes per SID, works on anything already doing MPLS, mature silicon. SRv6: SIDs are 128-bit IPv6 addresses, so it's routable IPv6 end-to-end with no MPLS data plane at all — attractive on a greenfield IP network — and supports network programming (the SID encodes a function, not just a location). Cost: 40-byte IPv6 outer + 8-byte SRH + 16 bytes per SID, so an explicit path is far heavier. uSID/C-SID compresses several 16-bit micro-SIDs into one 128-bit carrier and brings that down substantially.

The decision isn't nanoseconds of serialisation — it's MTU determinism on the WAN, whether the silicon does the transform at line rate, and operational familiarity. On a network that has never run MPLS, SRv6's "no MPLS data plane" argument is genuinely strong; on one already MPLS-capable it rarely justifies itself.

AB11. Inter-AS MPLS options A, B, C — and what SR does instead.

Option A (back-to-back VRF) — a sub-interface per VRF at the border, plain IP between ASes. Simple, auditable, scales terribly. Option B — ASBRs exchange VPNv4 with labels, no VRFs at the border; scales better but the ASBR holds all the VPN state. Option C — BGP-LU for the loopbacks plus multi-hop MP-eBGP for VPNv4 between route reflectors; ASBRs carry no VPN state. Scales best, weakest boundary control.

The SR answer: a binding-SID at the domain boundary. Each domain keeps its own SRGB and IGP; the headend pushes the remote domain's binding-SID, and the border expands it into that domain's path. Neither domain needs the other's SID space or topology — you get Option-C-like scaling with an explicit, auditable handoff. This is directly the migration deck's Option B.

AB12. MTU and MPLS — what breaks?

Every label is 4 bytes, so a 3-deep stack is 12 bytes off your payload; a VPN packet is transport + service label at minimum. Add a VXLAN or IPsec overlay on top and you're 50–100 bytes down.

The failure it causes is the PMTU black hole: the CE sets DF, something downstream can't fit it, the ICMP is filtered, and large transfers hang while pings succeed. The real fix is a core MTU of 9000+ so encapsulation is never the constraint, with MSS clamping at the edge as the mitigation. Ties directly to the pcap section.

AB13. TTL handling — uniform, pipe, short-pipe. Why does anyone care?

Uniform — IP TTL is copied into the label at imposition and back out at disposition, so the MPLS hops are visible to traceroute and each decrements. Pipe/short-pipe — the label gets its own TTL (255) and the IP TTL isn't decremented across the core, so the whole MPLS cloud looks like one hop — the topology is hidden from the customer.

Why it matters: it's a deliberate visibility versus opacity choice. Service providers hide the core; an internal network usually wants uniform so traceroute actually works when you're debugging at 3am. This is also why MPLS traceroute is odd — ICMP generated mid-LSP is forwarded to the tail and comes back the long way.

AB14. ECMP through an MPLS core — how does hashing work and what's the trap?

A P router sees labels, not the IP payload, so classic hashing had to guess: peek past the bottom of stack and test the first nibble — 0x4/0x6 implies IPv4/IPv6, so hash on that. That heuristic misfires on pseudowires where the payload is Ethernet that happens to start with 4 or 6, which silently reorders traffic within a flow.

Fixes: Entropy Label (RFC 6790) — the ingress computes a hash of the real flow and inserts it as a label (with ELI, label 7) so P routers hash on a field that's genuinely flow-unique; or a Flow-Aware Transport / control word on pseudowires to suppress the guessing. Cost: the entropy label is another label, so it eats MSD budget.

AB15. How do you troubleshoot a broken LSP?

Layer by layer, saying what each rules out: (1) IGP — is the FEC/loopback reachable at all? MPLS follows the IGP, so an IGP problem is not an MPLS problem. (2) Label binding — does the local box have a label for the FEC, and did the downstream advertise one (show mpls ldp bindings / the SR label from the IGP)? (3) Forwarding table — is the label actually in the LFIB with the right outgoing label and interface? (4) LSP ping/traceroute (RFC 4379/8029) — this is the tool people forget: it validates the label path specifically, and unlike ICMP it detects a broken LSP that IP still reaches via a fallback path. (5) MTU along the path. (6) For SR: SRGB consistency and whether the prefix-SID index resolves to the label you expect.

The distinguishing insight: IP reachability succeeding tells you nothing about the LSP — that's exactly the failure mode LSP-ping exists for.

AB16. You're asked: "we run plain IP with OSPF and BGP — why would we introduce SR at all?" Give the honest answer.

Start with what it isn't: it isn't load balancing (BGP DMZ link-bandwidth already does UCMP on plain IP), and it isn't raw speed. Saying that first buys you credibility for the rest.

The real case: (1) TI-LFA — guaranteed sub-50ms, 100%-coverage, microloop-free repair for any topology, which plain IGP reconvergence cannot give you. (2) Explicit path control — steer a flow off shortest-path without per-hop state or a new protocol, which is impossible in plain IP and expensive in RSVP-TE. (3) A programmable, controller-friendly substrate — BGP-LS north, SR Policy south, with the path expressed in the packet. (4) Inter-domain stitching via binding-SIDs without merging IGPs or leaking topology.

And the honest cost: a new SID/SRGB plan, MSD limits in silicon, a more complex troubleshooting story, and a team that must learn it. If the network's problem is none of those four things, the answer is don't do it — and being willing to say that is what makes the recommendation credible when you do make it.

AC · MULTICAST — QUIZ-ROUND DEPTH (cold source, added 2026-07-22)

Section K covers the market-data framing. This is the protocol mechanics. Your carrier IPTV SSM background is the asset here — it's the same zero-loss bar as market data.

AC1. Multicast addressing — the ranges and the layer-2 gotcha.

224.0.0.0/4 overall. 224.0.0.0/24 link-local control (never forwarded — 224.0.0.5/6 OSPF, .13 PIM, .1/.2 all-hosts/all-routers). 232.0.0.0/8 = SSM. 239.0.0.0/8 = administratively scoped/private (the multicast RFC 1918). 233/8 GLOP.

The gotcha: an IP group maps to MAC 01:00:5e + the low 23 bits of the group. The group has 28 significant bits, so 32 different groups collide onto one MAC — e.g. 239.1.1.1 and 239.129.1.1. A host joining one receives the other's traffic at L2 and discards it in software — wasted CPU and a genuinely confusing capture. It's why you don't allocate group addresses that differ only above the low 23 bits.

AC2. IGMP v1/v2/v3 — what does each add, and why does v3 matter to you?

v1 — joins only, no leave; membership aged out by timeout. v2 — adds leave-group and a group-specific query, so departure is fast; adds querier election (lowest IP wins). v3 — adds source filtering: INCLUDE(S,G) and EXCLUDE lists.

v3 is what makes SSM possible — the receiver names the source, so the network can build an (S,G) tree straight away with no RP and no shared tree. For market data that's the whole game: the source is known and fixed, so there is nothing to discover.

AC3. IGMP snooping and the querier — the most common multicast fault in a DC.

Snooping lets a switch listen to IGMP and forward a group only to ports that joined, instead of flooding it to the whole VLAN. But snooping entries age out, and they're refreshed by queries — which come from a router. On a pure L2 VLAN with no router, there's no querier, so entries expire and multicast stops after a couple of minutes, or floods everywhere, depending on the platform.

Signature: works, then dies; or works on some switches and not others. Fix: configure an IGMP snooping querier on the VLAN. Confirm it by disabling snooping — if it starts flooding and works, that's snooping, not routing.

AC4. PIM modes — name them and say which are actually deployed.

Dense mode — flood-and-prune, periodic reflood. Dead; never design with it. Sparse mode (ASM) — explicit joins toward an RP, shared tree then switchover. The general-purpose answer. SSM — (S,G) only, no RP, no shared tree; the answer for market data and IPTV. Bidir — shared tree in both directions, no source registration, for many-to-many (trading floors with lots of talkers); scales by removing (S,G) state entirely at the cost of never using the optimal path.

Note PIM is protocol-independent — it doesn't build its own topology, it relies on the unicast RIB for RPF. That dependency is the source of most multicast faults.

AC5. Walk ASM end to end: receiver joins, source starts. Every step.

(1) Receiver sends IGMP join for G. (2) Its last-hop router creates **(\*,G) and sends a PIM join toward the RP — hop by hop, building the shared tree (RPT). (3) The source starts sending; its first-hop router encapsulates the data in PIM Register messages unicast to the RP. (4) The RP decapsulates, forwards down the shared tree, and sends a PIM join toward the source to build (S,G). (5) Once native traffic arrives, the RP sends Register-Stop and the tunnelling ceases. (6) The last-hop router now sees the source's address, and when traffic crosses the SPT threshold (default: the first packet, on most platforms) it joins (S,G) directly toward the source — the SPT switchover — then prunes** itself off the shared tree.

Being able to name Register / Register-Stop and the switchover with the prune is the difference between having read it and having run it.

AC6. RPF — what is it and what breaks it?

Reverse Path Forwarding: a multicast packet is accepted only if it arrived on the interface the unicast routing table would use to reach the source. Otherwise it's dropped silently. It's the loop-prevention mechanism for a tree with no TTL-based safety.

What breaks it: asymmetric routing — the traffic arrives one way but the route to the source points another; a routing change that moves the source's best path; redistribution or a policy that makes the source reachable via a different interface than the physical path. The fix is either fixing the unicast path or a static mroute/RPF override — and if you're reaching for the override, the design is usually wrong.

Say the diagnostic out loud: "multicast forwarding follows the unicast route to the source, so I'd check the unicast path before touching PIM."

AC7. RP placement and redundancy — how do you not have a single point of failure?

Discovery mechanisms: static (simple, deterministic, and honestly fine when you control both ends), Auto-RP (Cisco, uses 224.0.1.39/.40), BSR (standards-based). Redundancy: Anycast-RP — the same RP address on multiple routers, with MSDP between them to exchange Source-Active messages so each knows the other's sources; receivers reach the nearest by unicast routing, and if one dies, routing simply converges to the other. (PIM Anycast-RP, RFC 4610, does the same without MSDP.)

The senior answer to "how do you protect the RP": use SSM and delete the RP from the design. For known, fixed sources — which is every market-data feed — the RP is a failure mode you chose to have.

AC8. SSM versus ASM — make the case in a finance context.

SSM has no RP, no shared tree, no register process, and no switchover. The receiver signals IGMPv3 INCLUDE (S,G), and the last-hop router builds the source tree immediately. That deletes an entire class of failure: RP unreachable, RP failover, MSDP problems, register tunnelling, the RPT→SPT transition glitch, and the (\*,G) state in the core.

Why finance and IPTV converge on it: the source is known and static, so the discovery ASM exists to provide has no value. You get the shortest path from the first packet, deterministically, and A/B feeds are trivially two (S,G)s on disjoint paths. The zero-loss bar is the same one broadcast video lives by — a pixelated frame and a missed fill are the same category of unacceptable.

AC9. PIM DR and the Assert — what are they for?

On a LAN with two routers, the DR (highest priority, tie-break highest IP) is the one that sends joins toward the RP/source for local receivers and registers local sources — it prevents duplicate signalling. The Assert solves a different problem: if both routers end up forwarding the same (S,G) onto the same segment, receivers get duplicates. They exchange Assert messages and the winner (best metric to the source, tie-break highest IP) becomes the sole forwarder.

Worth knowing they're separate elections with different criteria — DR is about signalling, Assert is about forwarding, and confusing them is a common slip.

AC10. Multicast over an EVPN/VXLAN fabric — what are the options?

Ingress replication (head-end replication) — the ingress VTEP makes a unicast copy per remote VTEP. Simple, no multicast in the underlay, but bandwidth scales with VTEP count — fine for BUM, bad for a real high-rate feed. Underlay multicast — the VNI maps to an underlay group and the fabric builds a real tree; efficient, but now you're running PIM in the underlay too. OISM / EVPN Route Type 6 (SMET) carries IGMP state in BGP so replication is selective.

Your position stays what's in section K: for market data, keep it on a dedicated PIM-SSM plane off the fabric. Don't tunnel a deterministic A/B feed through a fabric whose replication behaviour is a design compromise.

AC11. "The receiver gets nothing." Troubleshoot it in order.

(1) Is the source actually sending? Capture at the source. Astonishingly often the answer. (2) TTL — applications must set the multicast TTL explicitly; the default is 1, so it never leaves the host. Classic and worth naming early. (3) Did the receiver join? IGMP report in a capture at the host; and did it join the right (S,G) for SSM, and bind to the right interface (a multi-homed host joining on the wrong NIC). (4) Snooping/querier at L2 (AC3). (5) RPF at each router (AC6). (6) mroute state — walk it hop by hop from the receiver toward the source: does each router have the (S,G)/(\*,G) with the right incoming interface and a non-empty outgoing list? The hop where the OIL goes empty is your fault point. (7) For ASM: is the RP reachable, and did registration complete? (8) Boundaries/scoping — an administratively-scoped boundary or a TTL threshold on an interface deliberately dropping it.

The structure — source → host stack → L2 → RPF → hop-by-hop state — is the answer; the individual commands are vendor trivia.

AC12. mVPN in one answer — how does multicast cross an MPLS/SR core?

The core must carry a tree, not just unicast tunnels. Draft-Rosen (profile 0) built a GRE-based default MDT — every PE joined it, so all PEs got all traffic regardless of interest, plus a data MDT for high-rate streams. NG-MVPN replaced it: BGP auto-discovery (address family mvpn) to find PEs and signal C-multicast joins, with the provider tunnel being P2MP RSVP-TE, mLDP, or ingress replication.

In SR terms: the modern equivalents are Tree-SID (SR P2MP) — the controller computes the replication tree and installs it as SIDs, keeping SR's "no protocol in the core" property — or BIER, which is genuinely stateless: the packet header carries a bitstring of destination BFRs and routers replicate on it, with no per-tree state anywhere. If asked "what's next for multicast", BIER is the answer that shows you're current.

AD · GENERAL NETWORKING — THE CATCH-ALL (cold source, added 2026-07-22)

Weighted to what a trading firm actually probes: where latency comes from, layer 2, buffers, and the physical layer.

AD1. Build a latency budget for a link. What are the components, and which dominate?

Propagation — light in glass is ~200,000 km/s, so ≈5 µs per km, and that number is immovable: it's why colocation exists and why route mileage is the first thing you argue about. Serialisation — time to clock the bits out: a 1500-byte frame is 12,000 bits, so 1.2 µs at 10G, 120 ns at 100G. Switching — ~350 ns–1 µs per hop cut-through; more store-and-forward. Queueing — unbounded and the only variable one, which is why it's the enemy. Host stack — typically the largest single contributor, and the reason for kernel bypass.

The framing that lands: propagation is physics, serialisation and switching are engineering, queueing is the only one that's a choice — so low-latency design is mostly about never queueing.

AD2. Cut-through versus store-and-forward.

Store-and-forward buffers the whole frame, validates the FCS, then forwards — so latency scales with frame size and corrupt frames are dropped at that hop. Cut-through forwards as soon as it has read the destination MAC (~64 bytes), so latency is frame-size-independent and much lower — but it will propagate a corrupt frame, since the FCS hasn't been read yet (a "runt"/error frame forwarded onward).

Nuance worth having: cut-through cannot apply across a speed mismatch (10G→100G forces buffering) or when the egress port is busy, so a "cut-through switch" is store-and-forward whenever there's contention. Trading fabrics take the corrupt-frame risk for the latency.

AD3. FEC — why does a trading desk care?

Forward Error Correction (RS-FEC, mandatory on 25G/100G over most media) corrects bit errors but adds latency — roughly 100–250 ns per hop for RS-FEC (RS-528/544), which on a multi-hop path is real money at HFT scale. Some 25G modes allow FEC off or the lighter BASE-R/Firecode FEC over short, clean links.

The trade-off to state: FEC off = lower latency but you must have the link budget and error rate to support it; turn it off on a marginal link and you get CRC errors, which cut-through will happily forward onward. It's also a common interop trap — FEC mode must match on both ends or the link won't come up, and the autonegotiated default differs by vendor and cable type.

AD4. Why is spanning tree unwelcome in a modern fabric, and what replaced it?

STP's job is loop prevention by blocking links — so you pay for capacity you can't use, convergence is slow (RSTP is seconds, classic STP 30–50), and it's fragile: a single misbehaving device can cause an estate-wide topology change. TCNs flush MAC tables, causing floods.

Replaced by L3 to the leaf with ECMP (no L2 loops to prevent — the routing protocol handles multipath), and where L2 must extend, EVPN/VXLAN with L3 underlay, or MLAG/ESI-LAG so a dual-homed host uses both links actively. Keep STP configured as a backstop with BPDU guard on edge ports — you want it to catch someone plugging a switch into an access port, not to be your topology mechanism.

AD5. MLAG/ESI-LAG — what problem, and what's the risk?

A host wants two active uplinks to two different switches. LACP expects one peer, so the two switches must present a single system ID. MLAG (vendor-proprietary, with a peer-link and keepalive) or EVPN ESI-LAG (standards-based, using Ethernet Segment routes, no peer-link needed).

The risk is split-brain: peer-link fails but both switches are alive, both think they own the LAG, and you get duplicate/looped traffic. Mitigations: a separate keepalive path out-of-band from the peer-link, and orphan-port/suspend behaviour on the secondary. Ask about split-brain unprompted — it's the thing that actually bites.

AD6. Shaping versus policing, and where does each belong?

Policing drops (or re-marks) anything over rate immediately — no buffering, no added delay, but it's brutal to TCP because it drops mid-window and triggers retransmits. Shaping buffers excess and releases it at rate — smoother, TCP-friendlier, but it adds delay and needs memory.

Placement: police on ingress (you can't shape what you haven't received; policing is how you enforce a contract), shape on egress (typically to a downstream link's rate to avoid handing the next box a burst it will drop). For a latency-sensitive class you do neither — you give it strict priority with a policer only as a safety valve, because shaping a market-data feed means adding queueing delay to it, which is the one thing you're trying to avoid.

AD7. Buffers — is deep good?

No — deep buffers are a trade, not a virtue. Deep buffers absorb incast and speed mismatches without loss (good for storage, big-data, WAN edges) but they permit bufferbloat: packets sit in queue rather than being dropped, so latency and jitter rise and TCP takes longer to learn. Shallow-buffer, low-latency silicon drops earlier and keeps delay predictable — which a trading fabric prefers, because a dropped market-data packet is recovered from the B feed, but a delayed one poisons every downstream decision.

The right answer is match buffer to workload, and use ECN to signal congestion before the queue is full rather than relying on drop or depth.

AD8. Explain incast.

Many senders, one receiver, simultaneously — the classic pattern is a distributed query or a storage read fanning out to N nodes that all reply at once. The aggregate instantaneously exceeds the receiver's port rate, the egress buffer overflows in microseconds, and you get loss even though average utilisation is low.

Why it's nasty: it's synchronised, so it defeats statistical multiplexing, and it's invisible to utilisation graphs (see the microburst question, Y12). Mitigations: deeper buffers or ECN/DCTCP so senders back off before the drop, application-level jitter/staggering of the responses, and higher-rate uplinks. It's also the reason RoCE needs a lossless fabric — RDMA reacts far worse to loss than TCP does.

AD9. ARP, gratuitous ARP, proxy ARP — and where each causes trouble.

ARP resolves IP→MAC on a segment. Gratuitous ARP is an unsolicited announcement — used legitimately for failover (a VIP moving to a new box tells everyone to update their table) and for duplicate-address detection; also the mechanism behind ARP spoofing. Proxy ARP is a router answering on behalf of a remote host — occasionally a migration crutch, but it hides real topology, bloats ARP tables, and creates black holes when the router's own path changes. Default position: proxy ARP off.

The operational trap: ARP cache timeout versus MAC-table ageing mismatch — if the ARP entry outlives the MAC entry, the switch has to flood every frame to a silent host until it speaks again. Classic in fabrics with long-idle hosts.

AD10. Anycast — how does it work and what breaks?

The same address advertised from multiple locations; routing delivers each client to the topologically nearest instance. Perfect for stateless services — DNS is the canonical case — and it gives you failover for free: withdraw the route and traffic re-homes.

What breaks: anything stateful. A routing change mid-session moves a client to a different instance that has no state, so TCP sessions reset. Also traffic distribution is decided by routing, not by load, so you can't balance it, and debugging is confusing because two users hitting "the same server" hit different machines. In networking terms the same idea appears as anycast-SID and anycast-RP, both for exactly this reason: nearest-instance selection with automatic failover.

AD11. IPv6 — the differences that actually matter operationally.

No broadcast (multicast instead); NDP replaces ARP and runs over ICMPv6, so filtering ICMPv6 breaks the network — unlike IPv4 where blocking ICMP is merely rude. No fragmentation by routers — only the source fragments, so PMTUD is mandatory, which makes the ICMP black-hole failure far more damaging. SLAAC via router advertisements as an alternative to DHCPv6. A fixed 40-byte header with extension headers rather than options, which is friendlier to hardware parsing.

The one to name for SR: SRv6 uses the routing extension header (SRH) — so SRv6 is only possible because IPv6 has that extensibility, and the 128-bit address is what lets a SID encode both location and function.

AD12. How do you actually load-balance across parallel paths, and how does it go wrong?

A hash over the 5-tuple (plus often ingress port / MPLS labels / VXLAN outer source port) picks a member. It's per-flow, not per-packet, deliberately — per-packet would reorder and TCP would collapse.

Failure modes: polarisation — every layer of the fabric uses the same hash and the same fields, so flows that collide at one layer collide at every layer, leaving links idle while others congest (fix: per-layer hash seeds). Elephant flows — a single large flow can't be split at all, so one link saturates while the average looks fine. Poor entropy — a tunnelled or encapsulated workload where all traffic shares an outer 5-tuple, so everything hashes to one path; that's exactly what the entropy label (MPLS) and the VXLAN outer source port exist to fix. Uneven member counts after a failure change the modulo, rehashing flows onto new paths (resilient hashing limits the disruption to the flows that must move).

AD13. Where does jitter come from, and why does it matter more than latency to a trading system?

Sources: queueing (the dominant one — variable by definition), interrupt coalescing and scheduling on the host, retransmission and recovery, path changes between packets, and variable serialisation with mixed frame sizes.

Why it matters more: a consistent latency can be modelled and compensated for; a variable one cannot. A system with 100 µs ±1 µs is more usable than one with 60 µs ±40 µs, because the tail sets your risk, not the mean. This is also why you report p99/p99.9, never averages — and why "we added bandwidth" doesn't fix a jitter complaint.

AD14. A user reports "the network is slow." Structure your diagnosis.

Locate before solving. (1) Scope — one host, one segment, one application, or everyone? That single question eliminates most of the search space. (2) Slow how — throughput, latency, or intermittent loss? They have completely different causes and people say "slow" for all three. (3) When did it start, and what changed — the honest first suspect is always a change. (4) Layer by layer, bottom up: physical (errors, CRC, optic levels, flaps), L2 (duplex, MTU, MAC/ARP), L3 (path, asymmetry, MTU/PMTU), transport (window, retransmits, zero-window), application/host (CPU, socket buffers, disk). (5) Get evidence, not opinion — interface counters, a capture at two points, and the p99 rather than the average.

State the discipline explicitly: "I'd establish where the problem is before I touch anything, because a fix applied to the wrong layer creates a second fault."

AE · DESIGN + FAILURE SCENARIOS (cold source, added 2026-07-22)

Scenarios, not method — the method deck is design-round-method.html. For each: the questions you ask before designing, the shape of the answer, and the trap.

AE1. "Design the network for a new research cluster — a few thousand GPUs."

Ask first: how many GPUs and what interconnect (RoCEv2 or InfiniBand)? Rail-optimised or standard leaf-spine? Is storage on the same fabric? What's the oversubscription tolerance — training is bandwidth-hungry and loss-intolerant, not latency-critical in the trading sense. Growth horizon?

Shape: non-blocking (1:1) leaf-spine, or rail-optimised so each GPU's NIC rail has its own leaf — that keeps all-reduce traffic on one hop where possible. L3 to the leaf, eBGP underlay, ECMP. RoCEv2 needs a lossless class: PFC on the RoCE priority plus ECN/DCQCN doing the real work, with PFC as the last resort. 9000 MTU. Separate storage and management paths. Uplink bandwidth sized on the collective pattern, not on average utilisation.

Trap: designing it like an enterprise DC. The traffic is synchronised all-to-all bursts, not random client-server, so statistical multiplexing assumptions and average-utilisation sizing are both wrong. Name that.

AE2. "Design the WAN connecting three research DCs and two trading colos."

Ask first: what actually crosses it — bulk research data, or anything on the trade path? What's the latency requirement per leg, and is any of it latency-critical or just latency-sensitive? Diverse fibre available? Who owns the capacity — dark fibre, waves, or carrier IP?

Shape: treat research/inter-DC and trading as different networks with different rules. Research WAN: high bandwidth, engineered paths, richer policy — this is where SR earns its place (TI-LFA, explicit paths, binding-SID stitching). Trading colos: short, simple, deterministic, minimum state, dual diverse paths, no clever engineering. eBGP between sites for hard boundaries, IGP inside. Diversity is a physical-layer property — two circuits from two carriers sharing one duct is one circuit.

Trap: applying one design to both. The whole point is that they have opposite requirements, and saying so is the answer.

AE3. "Design market-data distribution from the venue to the consumers."

Ask first: how many venues and feeds, what rates and burst profile, how many consumers, A/B feeds available, is arbitration in the app or the network, and what's the recovery mechanism (snapshot/retransmit)?

Shape: PIM-SSM on a dedicated plane — no RP, IGMPv3 (S,G), deterministic SPT from the first packet. A and B feeds on physically disjoint paths with no shared fate (different switches, different fibre, ideally different rooms), arbitration at the host. Shallow-buffer low-latency switching, cut-through, and no shaping or policing on the feed. Line-rate capable everywhere — you size for burst, not average, because opens and news events are 10–100× normal. Monitoring on sequence-gap detection and p99 latency, not utilisation.

Trap: carrying it over the general fabric "because we have capacity." Capacity isn't the requirement — determinism and independence of the two feeds are.

AE4. "A spine fails in a 4-spine fabric. Walk through what happens."

Immediately: BFD detects it in milliseconds, eBGP sessions to that spine drop, each leaf withdraws that next-hop and keeps the other three in the FIB — with ECMP installed, no reconvergence is needed for the surviving paths, just a member removed from the group. Traffic continues at 75% of fabric capacity.

The real questions to raise: (1) Was it sized for N−1? If four spines at 80% utilisation lose one, three now carry 107% and you're dropping — capacity planning is the actual answer, not convergence. (2) Resilient hashing so removing a member doesn't rehash every flow. (3) During the maintenance version of this, drain gracefully (raise metric / AS-path prepend / graceful shutdown) rather than yanking the link.

Trap: answering only "ECMP handles it." The interesting content is the N−1 capacity question.

AE5. "Your RP fails and market data stops. Post-mortem it."

Immediate: with ASM, receivers on the shared tree lose the path to the RP; existing (S,G) flows that already switched to the SPT may survive, which is why the failure often looks partial and confusing. Anycast-RP/MSDP would have made it a non-event.

The post-mortem answer they want: the fix isn't a better RP, it's not having one. Move to SSM — the sources are known and static, so ASM's discovery mechanism was providing nothing while adding a failure domain. Then: why was a single RP in the path of a revenue-critical feed, and what else in that path has the same property?

Trap: answering with RP redundancy only. Redundancy for a component you didn't need is the weaker answer.

AE6. "Intermittent packet loss, nobody can find it. How do you hunt it?"

Bound it first: which flows, which direction, what rate, and is it correlated with load or time? Loss that correlates with load is congestion; loss that doesn't is physical or a control-plane/policing problem.

Then: interface error counters at every hop (CRC/FCS = physical — optic, cable, dirty connector; input discards = buffer; output drops = congestion). Microburst hunting — poll queue depth/drops rather than utilisation, or capture with hardware timestamps and graph at millisecond resolution. Asymmetric path — capture at two points and confirm the return path. Control-plane policing dropping legitimate traffic. Host-side — RcvbufErrors, ring-buffer overruns (ethtool -S), so it never was the network.

Trap: starting with a packet capture. Start with counters — they're already there, cover every hop, and tell you which hop to capture at.

AE7. "Half the traffic works, half doesn't, after a change."

This shape almost always means something is per-path or per-flow: an ECMP member that's broken (one bad link/optic in the bundle — flows hashing there die, others are fine), an asymmetric route where the return path differs and a stateful device drops it, an MTU mismatch on one path only, or one device in a pair with divergent config (an MLAG peer, one spine, one firewall in an HA pair).

Diagnostic that separates them: vary the flow tuple — if changing source port fixes it, it's per-flow hashing onto a broken member. If it's per-destination, it's routing. If it's only large packets, it's MTU.

Trap: treating it as intermittent. It isn't — it's deterministic per flow, and recognising that is most of the diagnosis.

AE8. "You must migrate the core with no downtime. How?"

Shape: build the new alongside the old (ships-in-the-night: new IGP/protocol running in parallel, no traffic). Move traffic with routing metrics, not with cables — cost-out one path, verify, cost-in. One failure domain at a time, starting where blast radius is smallest, never the core first. Every step needs a pre-check, a post-check, and a tested rollback — and a defined abort trigger agreed before the window, so the decision to roll back isn't taken by tired people at 4am. Soak between phases; the failures that matter often appear hours later under real load, not at cutover.

Then name the honest risks: transient asymmetry during the shift, MTU/label-stack changes, and the fact that rollback gets harder the further you go — so sequence the irreversible steps last.

Trap: presenting a plan with no rollback and no abort criteria. That's the single thing they're listening for.

AE9. "How do you know the network is healthy — what do you monitor?"

Not utilisation graphs. For a trading network: p99/p99.9 latency (not mean), jitter, sequence-gap counts on market-data feeds, queue depth and drop counters rather than average utilisation, interface error/CRC counts with trending (a slowly-degrading optic shows up here first), BGP/OSPF adjacency and route-count changes, and config drift versus intent.

The architectural point: streaming telemetry at sub-second, not SNMP polling at 30s — because the events that hurt you are microseconds long and a poll interval averages them away. Plus synthetic probes on the paths that matter, because an interface counter can't tell you the path is 40 µs slower than yesterday.

Trap: listing tools. They're asking what signals matter and why.

AE10. "Justify the SR migration to a sceptical engineer who likes the current plain-IP network."

Take the scepticism seriously — the current network works, and "it works" is a real argument. Concede first: SR is not faster, and it's not needed for load balancing (DMZ link-bandwidth already gives UCMP). Conceding what SR doesn't buy is what makes the rest credible.

Then the four real benefits: TI-LFA (guaranteed, 100%-coverage, microloop-free sub-50ms repair — unavailable in plain IP), explicit path control without per-hop state or a new protocol, a controller-friendly substrate (BGP-LS north, SR Policy south), and inter-domain stitching by binding-SID without merging IGPs. Then the costs: SID/SRGB planning, MSD limits in silicon, a harder troubleshooting story, and a team that has to learn it.

Close by scoping it: the research/inter-DC WAN, greenfield, leaving the trading path untouched — and say plainly that if none of those four benefits is a live problem, don't do it. Willingness to argue against your own proposal is what makes a recommendation trustworthy.

Trap: selling. A design round is testing judgement, not enthusiasm.
